\begin{abstract}
\noindent Roughly one‑third of U.S. primary care visits end with a specialist referral, yet little is known about how these referral links are first established. Focusing on primary care physicians (PCPs) who enter or relocate to a new market, we study the determinants of initial PCP–specialist networks using the universe of 2009–2018 Medicare fee‑for‑service claims across three distinct specialty areas, namely orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. A reduced‑form network‑formation model with two‑sided fixed effects shows that sharing a common physician practice is the dominant factor in forming initial referral links across all three specialties, followed by geographic proximity. Gender concordance plays a meaningful role in orthopedic surgery, is small but positive in dermatology, and is negligible for cardiology. An extension into the dynamics of referral links shows that organizational affiliation dissipates over the first four post‑move years in all three specialties, consistent with newly arrived physicians relying on convenience and familiarity when local information is limited and gradually shifting toward more information‑based matching.
\singlespacing

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Expert referrals shape the allocation of high‐stakes services in many industries, including finance, law, and healthcare; however, these referrals are often made in the face of significant uncertainty, where one expert may not be fully informed about the quality and skills of other experts in the market. In such settings, access to higher quality services can be facilitated or denied implicitly due to the existing network of the referring experts. Understanding decision making in these contexts remains an important area of research across several disciplines \citep{ching2013}. 

One area where decision making under uncertainty is particularly salient is in referrals from primary care physicians (PCPs) to specialists. When a PCP refers a patient to a specialist, that decision is made under potentially severe information frictions, and yet the referral matters significantly as it fixes the locus of subsequent treatment. The US healthcare system is increasingly reliant on such referrals, with as many as 30\% of visits to PCPs resulting in a referral to a specialist for additional care \citep{wright2022}, a near two-fold increase in referral rates over the preceding decade \citep{barnett2012aim}, and a heavy reliance on physician recommendations among patients \citep{chernew2021}. The initial referral of PCPs to specialists is therefore a critical step in the healthcare process, as it will determine the subsequent course of treatment for such patients. 

This paper provides some of the first causal evidence on the determinants of initial PCP–specialist referral networks. We exploit a natural experiment created by PCP movers, physicians who locate or relocate to a new geographic market and arrive with no local referral ties and must construct or reconstruct their networks from scratch. Using 100\% Medicare fee‐for‐service (FFS) claims from 2009–2018, we study referral formation across three specialty areas that span a range of clinical settings and physician demographics, namely orthopedic surgery (inpatient, predominantly male specialists), cardiology (office-based evaluation and management visits, predominantly male), and dermatology (office-based, roughly gender-balanced). For each specialty, we estimate a reduced‐form network‐formation model following \cite{jochmans2018} that allows for physician sorting across markets and unobserved (time-invariant) heterogeneity in referring PCPs and specialists.

% Results
Across all three specialties, practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral formation. The marginal effect is largest in orthopedic surgery, where sharing a practice group raises the referral probability by roughly 39 percentage points, followed by dermatology (32 percentage points) and cardiology (15 percentage points). Geographic proximity consistently lowers the referral probability by 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points) and small but positive in dermatology (1 percentage point), while negligible for cardiology, where over 85\% of specialists are male. Race, age, and experience concordance have economically small effects across all specialties.

For orthopedic surgery, where an external measure of surgeon quality is available, we also gauge the quality implications of these referral preferences by computing counterfactual referral probabilities under which the coefficient on each characteristic is set to zero. These exercises, presented in the supplemental appendix, suggest that the quality losses attributable to organizational and demographic matching are modest.



Finally, we extend our analysis by considering broader windows by which to define PCP-specialist referral networks (e.g., one year after relocating, two years, etc.). In all three specialties, the effect of practice affiliation attenuates as PCPs reside in their new market for longer periods, declining monotonically over four post-move years. Gender concordance also dissipates over time in orthopedic surgery, while geographic proximity remains a persistent factor. This pattern is consistent with organizational convenience playing a larger role when local information is limited.

% Contribution 1: Referral networks
Our analysis contributes to three distinct areas of economics and health policy. First, we contribute to the literature on physician referral networks. This literature typically considers physician networks in the context of shared patients \citep{landon2012, barnett2012mc, landon2018, linde2019}, wherein authors examine social networks of physicians defined as the set of physicians that see the same patients within a designated time period. These studies tend to envision an ``undirected'' physician network, in that the edges (i.e., connections between two physicians) reflect a two-way relationship with patients flowing from one physician to another in both directions. Our analysis more closely aligns with a smaller literature focusing on ``directed'' graphs in which referring physicians such as PCPs are connected to specialists \citep{agha2017, agha2018, zeltzer2020}. This literature typically considers physician networks as an input into healthcare production, while our analysis instead examines network formation itself. 

Our analysis aligns most directly with \cite{zeltzer2020}, who examines gender homophily in specialist referrals. However, our analysis remains distinct from \cite{zeltzer2020} in three key ways: 1) we study referral formation separately within three well-defined specialty areas, each with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, rather than pooling across a mix of conditions; 2) we focus on a PCP's initial referral network, avoiding the dynamics inherent in the evolution of such referrals over time; and 3) taking advantage of recent advances in the econometrics of networks \citep{jochmans2018, jochmans_fixedeffect_2019}, we are able to allow for a richer set of both PCP and specialist unobserved fixed effects in our analysis.

% Contribution 2: Referrals as a physician agency problem
Second, we contribute to the literature on physician agency with regard to referrals. This research extends the traditional role of physician agency to consider the role of physicians not just on the quantity and type of health care used, but also on the location of care \citep{baker2016, lin2025}. In the context of PCPs, the physician's decision-making authority is most salient in the referral process. Indeed, \cite{freedman2015} find that the PCP's recommendation is the most commonly cited reason from a patient in their selection of an oncologist, with similar results documented in \cite{barkowski2018} and \cite{chernew2021}. In an unpublished working paper, \cite{walden2016} examines how hospital acquisitions of primary care practices affect PCP referrals to the hospital. Like \cite{walden2016}, \cite{barkowski2018}, and \cite{chernew2021}, we consider PCP referrals as an important dimension of physician agency; however, rather than taking the PCP's referral network as given, our analysis seeks to determine how such networks are formed.

% Contribution 3: Understanding sources of supply-side variation in healthcare
Finally, we contribute to the broader study of sources of supply-side variation in healthcare. Overall, hospital and physician services constitute the two largest components of U.S. health expenditures and jointly accounted for nearly \$2 trillion in U.S. health spending in 2019 (52\% of total health expenditures); however, these expenditures are not distributed uniformly across geographic areas \citep{wennberg1973, gottlieb2010, miller2011, wennberg2003}. Rather, expenditures are characterized by areas of very high health care utilization alongside areas of very low utilization, and there is strong empirical evidence that a large share of this geographic variation is not driven by patient preferences \citep{zuckerman2010,finkelstein2016} or by differences in quality of care \citep{skinner1997,baicker2004ha}. Authors estimate that as much as 60\% of residual geographic variation in health care expenditures can be explained by provider behaviors as opposed to patient health care needs \citep{finkelstein2016}, and among this, physician practice style can explain as much as 50\% \citep{molitor2018}. There is also substantial variation in quality and costs across physicians within the same geographic area \citep{cooper2019, epstein2009, moy2020}. 

The conclusion from this literature is that a large share of otherwise unexplained variation in health care expenditures and quality of care is driven by provider behaviors such as physician practice patterns, facilitated by physicians' underlying influence on treatment decisions and location of care. However, policy solutions to remove this variation are elusive and require significant changes in physician behaviors. Our analysis considers referral patterns from PCPs to specialists as an important contributor to these existing inefficiencies. Given the PCP's role as a patient navigator, referral networks are a natural candidate for examining sources of and potential solutions to such variation, for two major reasons: 1) there is ample opportunity to improve patient health and lower health care costs if PCPs can better direct patients to more efficient and higher quality specialists; and 2) exploiting the PCP as a patient navigator offers an arguably more realistic and actionable way to reduce variation in care due to differential provider behaviors.

Ultimately, our focus on PCP referral networks is motivated by the search for practical and actionable health care policy to reduce variation in healthcare quality and utilization. Viewing physician treatment decisions as the source of variation necessarily implies that the solution to minimizing healthcare inefficiencies and improving quality lies in changing how a physician practices medicine. Unfortunately, an established body of research now demonstrates the many barriers to changing physician behaviors in this way \citep{wilensky2016}. This research largely confirms what physicians and other practicing clinicians have long known: it is very hard to change physician practice patterns. It is only more difficult to affect such change on a large scale, particularly with broad (i.e., non-individualized) healthcare policy. Our analysis instead envisions an opportunity to improve efficiency and quality not by changing what physicians do, but instead by changing which physicians do it. Because redirecting patients is typically easier than altering physicians’ practice styles, refining referral networks offers a low‐cost lever for improving health outcomes and reducing spending. 


\section{PCP Referral Networks}
\label{sec:model}

We discuss below the details of our network formation model and its econometric identification.

\subsection{A Model of Network Formation}
Our analysis follows a conceptual model in which a PCP's decision to initiate a referral relationship with a specialist is a function of three broad factors: 1) similarity in observable characteristics between the PCP and the specialist, denoted by $x_{ij}$; 2) the utility value of unobserved characteristics, denoted $a_{ij}$; and 3) idiosyncratic shocks that affect the formation of a referral link, denoted by $\varepsilon_{ij}$. Note that this setup considers the network formation decision of a PCP, not a given referral, and the outcome of interest is ultimately whether a PCP has any referral relationship with a given specialist. With this setup, a referral link between PCP $i$ and specialist $j$ will exist if the latent utility of such a relationship is positive:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:referral}
    y_{ij} = \mathbb{1} \left(x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij} - \varepsilon_{ij} \geq 0\right).
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:referral} is a reduced form representation of the referral network formation decision, where $y_{ij}$ takes the value of 1 if PCP $i$ refers to specialist $j$ and 0 otherwise. The vector of observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, captures several measures of practice and demographic homophily, including working in the same hospital and practice group, differential years of experience, differential distance between practices, and homophily in gender and race. 

Setting $a_{ij} = \alpha_{i} + \alpha_{j} - g(\xi_{i}, \xi_{j})$ allows for the presence of individual-specific unobserved heterogeneity for both PCPs and specialists, denoted by $\alpha_{i}$ and $\alpha_{j}$, respectively. The unobserved heterogeneity $\alpha_{i}$ reflects, for example, PCPs' productivity, and $\alpha_{j}$ similarly captures specialists' prestige or quality. The terms $\xi_{i}$ and $\xi_{j}$ reflect sorting into local markets, where similar preferences in local markets, denoted by $-g(\xi_{i}, \xi_{j})$, imply that PCP $i$ and specialist $j$ will be more likely to refer to each other.

Lastly, the error term $\varepsilon_{ij}$ captures the idiosyncratic shocks that affect the referral decision. Under the assumption that $\varepsilon_{ij}$ is distributed as a Type I extreme value, the probability of observing a referral from doctor $i$ to specialist $j$ is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:probability}
    \operatorname{Pr}(y_{ij} = 1 | x_{ij}) = \frac{e^{x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij}}}{1 + e^{x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij}}},
\end{equation}
therefore describing a network formation model that accounts for unobserved heterogeneity of doctors and specialists. What is more, the model acknowledges the geographical preferences that doctors and specialists have when forming referral networks. 

Link formation based on observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, and unobserved heterogeneity, $\alpha_{ij}$, describe two distinct features of real world networks: homophily and degree heterogeneity. These modeling choices mirror a large empirical literature on homophily and degree heterogeneity in physician networks.  Homophilous matching on practice environment and personal traits has been documented for patient–sharing ties \citep{barnett2012mc,landon_using_2013} and for gender–concordant referrals \citep{zeltzer2020,sarsons2023}. Degree dispersion, captured here by the PCP- and specialist-specific effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$, also follows that of recent network-formation models such as \cite{jackson2007} and \cite{graham_econometric_2017}. The key parameter of interest in Equation \ref{eq:probability} is $\beta$, which captures the effect of observed characteristics on the referral formation.


\subsection{Identification and Estimation}
\label{sec:empirical}

Our identification strategy follows \cite{jochmans2018} by differencing out the unobserved heterogeneity $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$ to recover the effect of observed characteristics $\beta$. The approach is a natural extension of the conditional logit model from \cite{chamberlain_analysis_1980} and conceptually similar to a difference-in-differences estimation. More specifically, we focus on quadruples $m=1,...,M$, with each quadruple consisting of two PCPs $(i, k)$ and two specialists $(j, l)$, and we compare the difference in referrals between these pairs.

For each quadruple, we transform the outcome $y_{ij}$ and characteristics $x_{ij}$ as
\begin{align*}
    \tilde{y}_m & = \frac{(y_{ij} - y_{il}) - (y_{kj} - y_{kl})}{2} \text{ and} \\
    \tilde{x}_m & = (x_{ij} - x_{il}) - (x_{kj} - x_{kl}).
\end{align*}
Under these transformations, identification of $\beta$ in Equation \eqref{eq:probability} derives entirely from $\tilde{y} \in \{-1, 1\}$. In other words, identification of the key parameters of interest comes entirely from discordant pairs wherein two senders (i.e., PCPs) rank the two receivers (i.e., specialists) differently. Conditioning on that discordant event removes the sender and receiver fixed effects and leaves a likelihood that depends solely on the covariate contrasts and the common parameter, $\beta$. Quadruples in which the two PCPs agree, or where one link is missing, contribute no information and are therefore discarded.

We can then estimate the parameter of interest $\beta$ by maximizing the log-likelihood function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
    L(\beta) = \sum_{m=1}^M 1\{\tilde{y}_m=-1\} \times \log F(\tilde{x}_m'\beta) \ + \ 1\{\tilde{y}_m=1\} \times \log (1-F(\tilde{x}_m'\beta)),
\end{equation}
where the transformed outcome $\tilde{y}$ resembles the process of differencing out the fixed effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$. For inference, we take into account the dependence between the quadruples of nodes and follow \cite{jochmans_fixedeffect_2019}.

While the Jochmans estimator delivers consistent estimates of $\beta$, it eliminates the fixed effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$ and therefore cannot produce predicted referral probabilities directly. To recover the full index $x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_i + \alpha_j$ needed for marginal-effect and counterfactual calculations, we employ a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, we estimate $\hat{\beta}$ from the quadruple-based conditional logit above. In the second stage, we hold $\hat{\beta}$ fixed and recover the doctor and specialist fixed effects by estimating a logit model with two-way fixed effects and the linear index $x_{ij}'\hat{\beta}$ entered as an offset. Because a large share of doctor--specialist pairs in the choice set exhibit no referral (a consequence of network sparsity), many fixed effects are not separately identified; however, the marginal effects computed on the non-separated subsample are stable across iteration counts, and we verify that the non-separated pairs are similar to the full choice set on observable characteristics. Both checks are presented in the supplemental appendix.

In addition to our econometric identification strategy developed by \cite{jochmans2018}, we also emphasize that established PCP-specialist links embed years of accumulated learning and habit formation. An analysis that pools all referrals may therefore conflate \emph{initial link formation} with the subsequent evolution of relationships, ultimately introducing potential bias in our estimate of $\beta$. To remove this source of bias, we focus on the initial referral decision. 

Focusing on physicians at the moment they enter a new HRR (e.g., PCP ``movers'') lets us observe referral choices made before such path dependence arises and isolates the forces (geographic proximity and social similarity) that govern where and how initial links form. The analytic sample of interest is therefore PCPs that moved to a non-contiguous HRR in the previous year. Under the assumption that PCPs do not move to a different HRR to refer to specialists based on observed clinical factors or the quality of available specialists in that market, this approach eliminates preferences on sorting, denoted by $-g(\xi_i, \xi_j)$ in Equation \eqref{eq:probability}, from the referral decisions.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

Our analysis draws on 100 percent Medicare fee-for-service (FFS) claims from 2009–2018. Referrals are constructed from Medicare Part A (inpatient) and Part B (carrier) files. Data on physician characteristics come from CMS Physician Compare, the Medicare Data on Provider Practice and Specialty (MD-PPAS), and the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES). Demographic data from Physician Compare are first available in 2012; however, we rely on those data for time-invariant physician characteristics such as year of medical school graduation.\footnote{CMS Physician Compare also includes data on which medical school a physician attended; however, these data are sparse and we exclude medical school from our analysis.} As such, our time frame is relatively unaffected by the late arrival of Physician Compare data.

We study referral formation across three specialty areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. These specialties span a range of clinical settings and referral processes. Orthopedic referrals are identified through inpatient admissions for planned and elective major joint replacement (Diagnosis Related Group codes), while cardiology and dermatology referrals are identified through new-patient evaluation and management (E\&M) office visits (Current Procedural Terminology codes). The three specialties also differ markedly in their demographic composition and organizational structure. Orthopedic surgeons are nearly all male (98\%), cardiologists are predominantly male (88\%), and dermatologists are roughly gender-balanced (56\% male). In the MD-PPAS data, 3.5\% of practice groups containing a PCP also include a cardiologist, 2.8\% include an orthopedic surgeon, and only 1.2\% include a dermatologist, reflecting the tendency of dermatologists to practice independently or in single-specialty groups.

Across all three specialties, we infer the referring PCP as the primary care physician with the highest number of office-based E\&M visits in the twelve months before the specialist encounter, following \cite{pham2009} and \cite{agha2017}, provided the patient saw that PCP at least twice.\footnote{An alternative approach uses the referring physician NPI (\texttt{rfr\_physn\_npi}) recorded on the specialist's claim. For orthopedic surgery, where we can assess this directly, this field is not populated in roughly 32\% of cases, and when populated, it most often lists the surgeon themselves (about 43\%) or a non-PCP specialist (about 7\%), with only 18\% listing a PCP. Our carrier-lookback method identifies a PCP for approximately 80\% of orthopedic procedures and, among cases where both methods identify a PCP, concordance exceeds 82\% in every year.} We limit the sample to PCPs and specialists each with at least 10 encounters over the full 2009–2018 period. Specialty-specific sample construction and summary statistics are discussed in Sections \ref{sec:ortho}--\ref{sec:derm}.

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:model}, our primary variables of interest consist of physician demographics, training, and practice characteristics. Since our analysis ultimately conditions on both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our key practice characteristic is geographic proximity, measured as the distance between zipcode centroids of the PCP’s primary practice location and that of the specialist. These data are available from MD-PPAS. We construct the differential age as well as years since medical school graduation as a measure of similarity in medical experience. These data come from CMS Physician Compare. We also construct measures of gender homophily, again from data in MD-PPAS.

Because physician race is not publicly available on a national scale, we impute physicians’ race by combining three complementary classifiers. First, we obtain name-based probabilities from NamePrism and from the WRU Bayesian surname-geocoding algorithm; second, we scrape profile photos from Zocdoc and use the DeepFace network to generate image-based probabilities. After aligning category labels across methods, we aggregate the separate predictions with a random-forest ensemble: a ``Full’’ model (using all three sources) attains 89\% accuracy for approximately 58,000 physicians, while a ``Name-based’’ model (names only) reaches 82\% accuracy for nearly 1 million physicians. Accuracy is assessed against verified race information drawn from Florida voter files, Texas medical-license records, and self-reported Zocdoc profiles. Full implementation details and accuracy are discussed in the supplemental appendix.

We flag a PCP as a \textit{mover} in the first year they practice in a new Hospital Referral Region (HRR). For every calendar year, we build a two-year window $\{t-1,t\}$, extract each doctor’s HRR in the MD-PPAS panel, and keep those whose HRR in year $t-1$ differs from the HRR in year $t$. To exclude cosmetic boundary shifts, we remove cases in which the origin and destination HRRs are geographic neighbors, identified from the Dartmouth Atlas HRR shapefile and its contiguity matrix. We further drop PCPs who register more than one HRR change inside the window, so every retained observation reflects a single, non-contiguous relocation. A PCP who relocates more than once across the panel (e.g., in 2012 and again in 2016) may appear in multiple annual cohorts; the independence assumption across quartets accommodates this since each cohort's quartets are formed within a single HRR-year cell. For our network formation model and subsequent estimation, a PCP is assumed to construct their referral network as a subset of all possible specialists in their HRR. Referral counts among any given PCP-specialist pair are on average small; as such, we define PCP-specialist links as any observed referral between a given PCP and specialist, effectively ignoring the intensive margin.

\section{Orthopedic Surgery}
\label{sec:ortho}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Major joint replacement is among the most common inpatient surgical procedures for Medicare beneficiaries. Because these procedures are planned and elective, the PCP’s referral to an orthopedic surgeon typically occurs well in advance of the surgery, making the referral decision relatively deliberate. Orthopedic surgery is also one of the most male-dominated specialties, with 98\% of surgeons in our data being male. Among practice groups that contain at least one PCP, 2.8\% also include an orthopedic surgeon.

From the Medicare FFS claims data, we identify eligible planned and elective orthopedic surgeries by DRG codes 461, 462, 469, 470, 480--489, and 507--511. We keep only cases in which the operation is performed by an orthopedic surgeon (per MD-PPAS specialty codes) and a referring PCP can be inferred. Our full referral data consist of approximately 566,000 PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 64,000 unique PCPs, and around 15,200 unique specialists. From 2010--2018, we identify 1,234 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-ortho} summarizes the orthopedic referral data, with Panel A presenting information on PCPs and Panel B on specialists. The median PCP refers to 2 distinct orthopedic surgeons, is 53 years old, and is male in 75\% of cases. Movers are younger (48 years), less male (66\%), and refer to fewer specialists immediately after relocating. From Panel B, specialists receive referrals from a median of 6 unique PCPs. Specialists receiving referrals from PCP movers are more likely to be white (92\% versus 90\%) and slightly more likely to be male (97.8\% versus 97.6\%) compared to the full data, despite PCP movers being less likely to be white males than the broader PCP population.

Figure \ref{fig:map-movers-ortho} presents the origin and destination HRRs for PCP movers in the orthopedic sample. The observed referral network is relatively sparse, with links representing 3.5\% of all potential PCP-specialist pairs within each HRR-year cell.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-ortho} contrasts observable concordance between established and non-established referral links. Organizational proximity and geography dominate: 18.9\% of linked pairs share a practice group (versus 2.8\% of non-links), and the average distance is 10 miles for established links versus 42 miles for non-links. Demographic concordance differs modestly between groups, with gender concordance at 66.6\% versus 65.4\% and race concordance at 75.5\% versus 71.4\%.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-ortho} summarizes the two-stage estimation results for orthopedic surgery. Columns (1)--(3) report the structural coefficients (log-odds) from the Jochmans estimator across three specifications, while columns (4)--(6) present the corresponding average marginal effects. We focus on the marginal effects.

Practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral choice. Sharing a practice group raises the referral probability by approximately 39 percentage points (95\% CI: 22 to 55), dwarfing all other characteristics. Roughly 37\% of PCP movers have at least one same-practice orthopedic surgeon in their choice set, and for those who do, referring within-practice may reflect organizational convenience (e.g., shared information systems, physical proximity within the practice). The supplemental appendix presents a robustness specification dropping practice affiliation entirely, isolating the ``reaching outside the practice'' decision. Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 5 percentage points per 5 miles. Gender concordance increases the probability by around 6 percentage points. Race concordance has a point estimate of 7 percentage points but is imprecisely estimated. Age and experience concordance are economically negligible.

\subsection{Dynamics}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-ortho} documents how PCP referral networks evolve as we widen the post-move window from one to four years. The average network expands from 1.7 distinct specialists in the move year to 3.9 by year 4. Within-practice referrals decline from 18.4\% to 14.7\%, and mean distance increases from 10.5 to 12.7 miles.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-ortho} presents the marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation dominates immediately after relocation but declines monotonically, falling from roughly 39 percentage points in the move year to approximately 15 percentage points by year 4. Gender concordance also dissipates over time. Geographic distance retains a persistent penalty throughout. To rule out compositional dilution as an explanation for this attenuation (since cumulative windows mechanically push shares toward population averages as the denominator grows), the supplemental appendix decomposes each window's links into persisting and newly formed ties. If attenuation were purely mechanical, new links in later windows would show concordance rates similar to those of persisting links; instead, they exhibit progressively lower organizational and demographic concordance, consistent with genuine behavioral change. The supplemental appendix also presents period-specific (non-cumulative) estimates, where the model is estimated separately on referrals made in each individual post-move year rather than cumulative windows, providing further evidence against compositional dilution.


\section{Cardiology}
\label{sec:cardio}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Cardiology referrals in our data are based on new-patient E\&M office visits (CPT codes 99201--99205) performed by cardiologists and clinical cardiac electrophysiologists. Unlike the surgical orthopedic referrals, these are initial consultations for evaluation rather than discrete procedures. Cardiology is predominantly male (88\% of specialists), though less so than orthopedic surgery. Among PCP-containing practice groups, 3.5\% also include a cardiologist, the highest co-location rate of the three specialties we study, reflecting the integration of cardiology into hospital-based and multispecialty group practices.

We identify 1.67 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 113,000 unique PCPs, and around 23,000 unique specialists. The larger sample reflects the broader volume of cardiology E\&M visits relative to orthopedic surgeries. From 2010--2018, we identify 5,071 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-cardioem} summarizes the cardiology referral data. PCPs refer to a median of 3 distinct cardiologists, compared to 2 for orthopedic surgery, consistent with the higher frequency of cardiology consultations. Movers are younger (46 years) and less male (59\%) than the overall PCP population.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-cardioem} contrasts established and non-established referral links. Same-practice rates are notably higher than in orthopedic surgery: 24.8\% of linked pairs share a practice group (versus 3.4\% of non-links). Distance patterns are similar, with established links averaging 11 miles versus 33 miles for non-links. Demographic concordance is lower overall than in orthopedic surgery, reflecting the more diverse composition of both PCPs and cardiologists in this sample.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-cardioem} presents the two-stage estimation results. Practice affiliation remains the dominant predictor, but the marginal effect is considerably smaller than in orthopedic surgery, at approximately 15 percentage points. Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 2 percentage points per 5 miles. Gender and race concordance are both negligible, with point estimates near zero. The small gender effect likely reflects the limited within-specialty gender variation, as 88\% of cardiologists in our data are male.

\subsection{Dynamics}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-cardioem} documents the evolution of cardiology referral networks. Networks expand more rapidly than in orthopedic surgery, from 2.4 specialists in the move year to 6.2 by year 4, reflecting the higher frequency of cardiology consultations. Within-practice referrals decline modestly from 24.8\% to 22.1\%.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-cardioem} presents the marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation attenuates over time, though the decline is less pronounced than in orthopedic surgery. Gender and race concordance remain near zero throughout. The link decomposition in the supplemental appendix confirms that this attenuation is not driven by compositional dilution.


\section{Dermatology}
\label{sec:derm}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Dermatology referrals are based on new-patient E\&M office visits, the same CPT codes used for cardiology. Dermatology is the most gender-balanced specialty in our analysis, with 56\% male specialists. Dermatologists also tend to practice independently or in single-specialty groups. Only 1.2\% of PCP-containing practice groups include a dermatologist, the lowest co-location rate of the three specialties.

We identify approximately 1.60 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 116,000 unique PCPs, and around 13,000 unique specialists. From 2010--2018, we identify 4,664 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-derm} summarizes the dermatology referral data. PCPs refer to a median of 3 distinct dermatologists, and movers are younger (46 years) and less male (56\%) than the overall PCP population. Dermatologists have the highest median in-degree of the three specialties (27 referring PCPs), consistent with fewer dermatologists serving larger catchment areas.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-derm} contrasts established and non-established links. Same-practice rates are the lowest of the three specialties, at 12.2\% of linked pairs versus 2.0\% of non-links. Distance patterns are again similar, with established links averaging 10 miles versus 29 miles for non-links.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-derm} presents the two-stage estimation results. Practice affiliation is again the dominant predictor, with a marginal effect of approximately 32 percentage points. Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 3 percentage points per 5 miles. Gender concordance is small but positive at approximately 1 percentage point, and race concordance is similarly small at less than 1 percentage point. Despite dermatology being the most gender-balanced specialty in our analysis, the gender concordance effect is much smaller than in orthopedic surgery.

\subsection{Dynamics}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-derm} documents the evolution of dermatology referral networks. Networks expand from 2.3 specialists in the move year to 5.9 by year 4. Within-practice referrals decline from 12.1\% to 10.3\%.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-derm} presents the marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation attenuates over time, declining from roughly 32 percentage points to approximately 20 by year 4. Gender and race concordance remain small throughout. As with the other specialties, the link decomposition in the supplemental appendix confirms that this attenuation is not driven by compositional dilution.


%%% CROSS-SPECIALTY COMPARISON

\section{Cross-Specialty Comparison}
\label{sec:cross-specialty}

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-cross} presents a forest plot of the full-specification marginal effects from Tables \ref{tab:logit-twfe-ortho}--\ref{tab:logit-twfe-derm}. Several patterns emerge.

First, practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral formation in all three specialties, but the magnitude varies substantially. The marginal effect is largest in orthopedic surgery (39 percentage points), followed by dermatology (32) and cardiology (15). This ordering does not simply mirror co-location rates. Cardiology has the highest share of same-practice links (24.8\%) yet the smallest marginal effect, while orthopedic surgery has an intermediate co-location rate (18.9\%) and the largest effect. The relationship between co-location prevalence and the marginal effect of practice affiliation is not monotonic, suggesting that institutional differences across specialties also shape how organizational ties translate into referral decisions.

Second, geographic proximity is a robust and persistent predictor across all three specialties, with marginal effects ranging from 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles.

Third, demographic concordance varies across specialties. Gender concordance is largest in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points), small in dermatology (1 percentage point), and negligible in cardiology. Notably, gender concordance does not scale with the degree of gender variation in the specialist pool. Orthopedic surgery, where specialists are nearly all male, shows the largest gender effect, while dermatology, with a roughly gender-balanced specialist pool, shows a much smaller effect. Race concordance is imprecisely estimated in all three specialties. Age and experience play no meaningful role anywhere.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-cross} presents the dynamics of marginal effects across specialties. The attenuation of practice affiliation over the first four post-move years is a consistent finding. The effect declines in all three specialties. This pattern is consistent with organizational convenience playing a diminishing role as PCPs spend more time in their new market.


%%% WELFARE

\section{Quality Reallocation}
\label{sec:welfare}

For orthopedic surgery, where an external measure of surgeon quality is available (the CMS Comprehensive Care for Joint Replacement Model and the National Quality Forum), we assess the quality implications of referral preferences by computing counterfactual referral probabilities when the coefficient on each characteristic is set to zero. These quality-reallocation exercises, presented in the supplemental appendix, suggest that the losses attributable to organizational and demographic matching are modest, consistent with the relatively small marginal effects for most covariates outside of practice affiliation. Quality measures are not available for the cardiology E\&M and dermatology samples, as these involve office-based consultations rather than discrete surgical procedures with well-defined outcome metrics.

The supplemental appendix also presents conditional logit results analogous to \cite{zeltzer2020}, where we condition on PCP and year fixed effects but not on specialist fixed effects. In general, effects are attenuated in these models relative to the two-stage estimates, and we discuss these differences and their practical implications in more detail there.


%%% DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This paper examines the determinants of initial referral networks between PCPs and specialists across three clinical areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. Our identification strategy exploits PCP movers in order to isolate the initial referral network from the evolution of such networks over time. We further employ the estimator in \cite{jochmans2018}, allowing for both PCP and specialist fixed effects in a quadruple-based conditional logit. We estimate effects using the 100\% Medicare FFS claims data from 2009 through 2018.

Across all three specialties, practice group affiliation is by far the largest contributor to link formation, with geographic proximity also emerging as a robust and persistent factor. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery but plays a smaller role in dermatology and is negligible for cardiology. Other measures of demographic concordance, including age, experience, and race, play at best a limited role. The effects of practice affiliation attenuate as the post-move horizon widens in all three specialties, suggesting that organizational convenience plays a diminishing role over time.

While we employ a different identification strategy and a richer econometric specification than \cite{zeltzer2020}, allowing for both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our baseline results for practice affiliation and gender concordance in orthopedic surgery are not too dissimilar, albeit with slightly smaller magnitudes. By examining three specialties with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, we show that the dominance of organizational ties in referral formation is not specific to any single clinical area, though the magnitude varies across specialties.

We conclude with three policy implications. First, while increasing horizontal integration of physician practices can have large negative consequences with regard to competitiveness and physician pricing \citep{koch2021}, our results suggest that, insomuch as mergers alter physician referrals, the quality implications of such referral changes are likely minor. Second, our results uncover the role of geographic proximity as a mediator for potential bias in referral patterns. If such biases were extended to the entire pool of specialists in an HRR, the quality of care consequences could be large; however, when limited to a narrow geographic subset of specialists, preferences for demographic homophily have less opportunity to negatively affect patient care. Finally, while organizational affiliation plays a dominant role in PCPs’ initial referral networks, these effects attenuate over time across all three specialties. Identifying the determinants of such network evolution over time remains an important area of future research.
