\begin{abstract}
\noindent Roughly one‑third of U.S. primary care visits end with a specialist referral, yet little is known about how these referral links are first established. Focusing on primary care physicians (PCPs) who enter or relocate to a new market, we study the determinants of initial PCP–specialist networks using the universe of 2009–2018 Medicare fee‑for‑service claims across three distinct specialty areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. A reduced‑form network‑formation model with two‑sided fixed effects shows that sharing a common physician practice is the dominant factor in forming initial referral links across all three specialties, followed by geographic proximity. Gender concordance plays a meaningful role in orthopedic surgery, is small but positive in dermatology, and is negligible for cardiology. Our analysis of the dynamics of referral links shows that organizational affiliation dissipates over the first four post‑move years, consistent with newly arrived physicians relying on convenience and familiarity when local information is limited and gradually shifting away from such factors over time.
\singlespacing

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Expert referrals shape the allocation of high‐stakes services in many industries, including finance, law, and healthcare; however, these referrals are often made in the face of significant uncertainty, where one expert may not be fully informed about the quality and skills of other experts in the market. In such settings, access to higher quality services can be facilitated or denied implicitly due to the existing network of the referring experts. Understanding decision making in these contexts remains an important area of research across several disciplines \citep{ching2013}. 

One area where decision making under uncertainty is particularly salient is in referrals from primary care physicians (PCPs) to specialists. When a PCP refers a patient to a specialist, that decision is made under potentially severe information frictions, and yet the referral matters significantly as it fixes the locus of subsequent treatment. The US healthcare system is increasingly reliant on such referrals, with as many as 30\% of visits to PCPs resulting in a referral to a specialist for additional care \citep{wright2022}, a near two-fold increase in referral rates over the preceding decade \citep{barnett2012aim}, and a heavy reliance on physician recommendations among patients \citep{chernew2021}. The initial referral of PCPs to specialists is therefore a critical step in the healthcare process. 

This paper provides some of the first credible causal evidence on the determinants of initial PCP–specialist referral networks and the evolution of such networks over time. To do so, we focus on referrals from PCPs who locate or relocate to a new geographic market and arrive with no previously observed local referral ties and must therefore construct or reconstruct their networks from scratch. Using 100\% Medicare fee‐for‐service (FFS) claims from 2009–2018, we study referral formation across three specialty areas that span a range of clinical settings and physician demographics, namely orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. PCP movers are observationally similar to non-movers on the characteristics we study, and we verify this directly in the supplemental appendix. For each specialty, we estimate a reduced‐form network‐formation model with two-sided fixed effects for both the referring PCP and the specialist. Standard conditional logit estimation of such models is inconsistent due to the incidental parameters problem, so we adopt the quadruple-based estimator of \cite{jochmans2018}, which differences out both sets of fixed effects. We then recover the fixed effects in a second stage in order to compute marginal effects on the probability scale. A simulation exercise, presented in the supplemental appendix, confirms that this two-stage procedure recovers the true marginal effects in a setting with known parameters.

% Results: Initial formation
Across all three specialties, practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of initial referral formation. The marginal effect is largest in orthopedic surgery, where sharing a practice group raises the referral probability by roughly 39 percentage points (95\% CI: 22 to 55), followed by dermatology (32 percentage points) and cardiology (15 percentage points). Geographic proximity consistently lowers the referral probability by 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points) and small but positive in dermatology (1 percentage point), while negligible for cardiology, where over 85\% of specialists are male. Race, age, and experience concordance have economically small effects across all specialties.

% Results: Dynamics
We also study how these referral networks evolve as PCPs spend more time in their new market. Estimating our network formation model separately over expanding post-move windows (one year, two years, etc.), we find that the effect of practice affiliation attenuates monotonically over four post-move years in all three specialties. Gender concordance also dissipates over time in orthopedic surgery, while geographic proximity remains a persistent factor. We further decompose each window's referral links into persisting and newly formed ties and find that newly formed links exhibit progressively lower organizational and demographic concordance, indicating genuine behavioral change rather than mechanical dilution. This pattern suggests that newly arrived physicians rely heavily on organizational convenience when local information is limited and gradually shift away from such factors over time.

% Results: Welfare
For orthopedic surgery, where discrete surgical episodes allow for well-defined quality measurement, we gauge the quality implications of these referral preferences by computing counterfactual referral probabilities under which the coefficient on each characteristic is set to zero. These exercises suggest that the quality losses attributable to organizational and demographic matching are modest, in line with the relatively small marginal effects for most covariates outside of practice affiliation. Quality measurement is more difficult for cardiology and dermatology, where the referral typically leads to an office-based consultation rather than a discrete procedure with well-defined outcome metrics.

% Contribution 1: Referral networks
Our analysis contributes to three distinct areas of economics and health policy. First, we contribute to the literature on physician referral networks. This literature typically considers physician networks in the context of shared patients \citep{landon2012, barnett2012mc, landon2018, linde2019}, wherein authors examine social networks of physicians defined as the set of physicians that see the same patients within a designated time period. These studies tend to envision an ``undirected'' physician network, in that the edges (i.e., connections between two physicians) reflect a two-way relationship with patients flowing from one physician to another in both directions. Our analysis more closely aligns with a smaller literature focusing on ``directed'' graphs in which referring physicians such as PCPs are connected to specialists \citep{agha2017, agha2018, zeltzer2020}. This literature typically considers physician networks as an input into healthcare production, while our analysis instead examines network formation itself. 

Our analysis aligns most directly with \cite{zeltzer2020}, who examines gender homophily in specialist referrals. However, our analysis remains distinct from \cite{zeltzer2020} in three key ways: 1) we study referral formation separately within three well-defined specialty areas, each with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, rather than pooling across a mix of conditions; 2) by focusing on PCP movers who must build referral networks from scratch, we can study both initial network formation and the subsequent evolution of referral relationships, separating the forces that govern first contact from those that sustain or reshape ties over time; and 3) taking advantage of recent advances in the econometrics of networks \citep{jochmans2018, jochmans_fixedeffect_2019}, we allow for a richer set of both PCP and specialist unobserved fixed effects.

% Contribution 2: Referrals as a physician agency problem
Second, we contribute to the literature on physician agency with regard to referrals. This research extends the traditional role of physician agency to consider the role of physicians not just on the quantity and type of health care used, but also on the location of care \citep{baker2016, lin2025}. In the context of PCPs, the physician's decision-making authority is most salient in the referral process. Indeed, \cite{freedman2015} find that the PCP's recommendation is the most commonly cited reason from a patient in their selection of an oncologist, with similar results documented in \cite{barkowski2018} and \cite{chernew2021}.\footnote{\cite{walden2016} also examines how hospital acquisitions of primary care practices affect PCP referrals to the hospital, providing further evidence that organizational structure shapes where patients are sent.} Like these studies, we consider PCP referrals as an important dimension of physician agency; however, rather than taking the PCP's referral network as given, our analysis seeks to determine how such networks are initially formed and how they evolve.

% Contribution 3: Understanding sources of supply-side variation in healthcare
Finally, we contribute to the broader study of sources of supply-side variation in healthcare. Overall, hospital and physician services constitute the two largest components of U.S. health expenditures and jointly accounted for nearly \$2 trillion in U.S. health spending in 2019 (52\% of total health expenditures); however, these expenditures are not distributed uniformly across geographic areas \citep{wennberg1973, gottlieb2010, miller2011, wennberg2003}. Rather, expenditures are characterized by areas of very high health care utilization alongside areas of very low utilization, and there is strong empirical evidence that a large share of this geographic variation is not driven by patient preferences \citep{zuckerman2010,finkelstein2016} or by differences in quality of care \citep{skinner1997,baicker2004ha}. Authors estimate that as much as 60\% of residual geographic variation in health care expenditures can be explained by provider behaviors as opposed to patient health care needs \citep{finkelstein2016}, and among this, physician practice style can explain as much as 50\% \citep{molitor2018}. There is also substantial variation in quality and costs across physicians within the same geographic area \citep{cooper2019, epstein2009, moy2020}. 

The conclusion from this literature is that a large share of otherwise unexplained variation in health care expenditures and quality of care is driven by provider behaviors such as physician practice patterns, facilitated by physicians’ underlying influence on treatment decisions and location of care. However, policy solutions to remove this variation are elusive and require significant changes in physician behaviors \citep{wilensky2016}. Our analysis considers referral patterns from PCPs to specialists as an important contributor to these existing inefficiencies. Given the PCP’s role as a patient navigator, referral networks are a natural candidate for examining sources of and potential solutions to such variation. If PCPs can better direct patients to more efficient and higher quality specialists, there is ample opportunity to improve patient health and lower health care costs. Moreover, because redirecting patients through better-informed referrals is typically easier than altering physicians’ established practice styles, refining referral networks offers a relatively low-cost lever for reducing spending and improving outcomes.


\section{PCP Referral Networks}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{A Model of Network Formation}
Our analysis follows a conceptual model in which a PCP's decision to initiate a referral relationship with a specialist is a function of three broad factors: 1) similarity in observable characteristics between the PCP and the specialist, denoted by $x_{ij}$; 2) the utility value of unobserved characteristics, denoted $a_{ij}$; and 3) idiosyncratic shocks that affect the formation of a referral link, denoted by $\varepsilon_{ij}$. Note that this setup considers the network formation decision of a PCP, not a given referral, and the outcome of interest is ultimately whether a PCP has any referral relationship with a given specialist. With this setup, a referral link between PCP $i$ and specialist $j$ will exist if the latent utility of such a relationship is positive:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:referral}
    y_{ij} = \mathbf{1} \left(x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij} - \varepsilon_{ij} \geq 0\right).
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq:referral} is a reduced form representation of the referral network formation decision, where $y_{ij}$ takes the value of 1 if PCP $i$ refers to specialist $j$ and 0 otherwise. The vector of observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, captures several measures of practice and demographic homophily, including working in the same hospital and practice group, differential years of experience, differential distance between practices, and homophily in gender and race. 

We parameterize the unobserved component as $a_{ij} = \alpha_{i} + \alpha_{j}$, where $\alpha_{i}$ and $\alpha_{j}$ are individual-specific effects for PCPs and specialists, respectively. The PCP effect $\alpha_{i}$ reflects, for example, a PCP's productivity or propensity to refer, and $\alpha_{j}$ similarly captures specialists' prestige or quality. In principle, physicians who practice in the same geographic area may also share unobserved preferences that make a referral link more likely, independent of their observable characteristics. We address this sorting concern through our research design rather than through additional model parameters, as discussed in Section \ref{sec:identification}.

Lastly, the error term $\varepsilon_{ij}$ captures idiosyncratic shocks that affect the referral decision. Under the assumption that $\varepsilon_{ij}$ follows a Type I extreme value distribution, the probability of observing a referral from PCP $i$ to specialist $j$ takes the logistic form,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:probability}
    \operatorname{Pr}(y_{ij} = 1 | x_{ij}) = \frac{e^{x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij}}}{1 + e^{x_{ij}'\beta + a_{ij}}},
\end{equation}
therefore describing a network formation model that accounts for unobserved heterogeneity of doctors and specialists. Geographic proximity enters the model through $x_{ij}$, which includes the distance between PCP and specialist practice locations.

Link formation based on observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, and unobserved heterogeneity, $\alpha_{ij}$, describe two distinct features of real world networks, namely homophily and degree heterogeneity. These modeling choices mirror a large empirical literature on homophily and degree heterogeneity in physician networks.  Homophilous matching on practice environment and personal traits has been documented for patient–sharing ties \citep{barnett2012mc,landon_using_2013} and for gender–concordant referrals \citep{zeltzer2020,sarsons2023}. Degree dispersion, captured here by the PCP- and specialist-specific effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$, also follows that of recent network-formation models such as \cite{jackson2007} and \cite{graham_econometric_2017}. The key parameter of interest in Equation \ref{eq:probability} is $\beta$, which captures the effect of observed characteristics on the referral formation.


\subsection{Identification}
\label{sec:identification}

Our identification strategy rests on two design choices. The first is a focus on PCP ``movers,'' physicians who relocate to a non-contiguous Hospital Referral Region (HRR) and therefore arrive in a new market with no previously observed local referral ties. Established PCP-specialist links embed years of accumulated learning and habit formation, so an analysis that pools all referrals may conflate initial link formation with the subsequent evolution of relationships, ultimately introducing potential bias in our estimate of $\beta$. Focusing on physicians at the moment they enter a new HRR lets us observe referral choices made before such path dependence arises and isolates the forces that govern where and how initial links form. Under the assumption that PCPs do not relocate to a specific HRR in order to refer to particular specialists, this design also helps to eliminate correlation between unobserved PCP and specialist characteristics that arises from shared location preferences. This assumption is plausible if, for example, physician relocations are driven by broader career opportunities, family considerations, or institutional recruitment rather than by the composition of the local specialist pool. A violation would require PCPs to select their destination HRR based on the demographic or practice characteristics of specialists in that market. If such selection were present, it would likely bias our estimates upward, as PCPs who sort into markets with demographically similar specialists would exhibit higher concordance than PCPs who relocate for unrelated reasons.

The second design choice is the use of discordant quadruples, following \cite{jochmans2018}. Each quadruple consists of two PCPs $(i, k)$ and two specialists $(j, l)$. We transform the outcome $y_{ij}$ and characteristics $x_{ij}$ as
\begin{align*}
    \tilde{y}_m & = \frac{(y_{ij} - y_{il}) - (y_{kj} - y_{kl})}{2} \text{ and} \\
    \tilde{x}_m & = (x_{ij} - x_{il}) - (x_{kj} - x_{kl}).
\end{align*}
This double-differencing is conceptually analogous to a difference-in-differences estimator. The first difference $(y_{ij} - y_{il})$ removes the PCP fixed effect $\alpha_i$ (much as within-unit differencing removes a unit fixed effect), and the second difference against $(y_{kj} - y_{kl})$ removes the specialist fixed effects $\alpha_j$ and $\alpha_l$. Under these transformations, identification of $\beta$ derives entirely from $\tilde{y} \in \{-1, 1\}$, that is, from quadruples in which the two PCPs rank the two specialists differently. Quadruples in which the two PCPs agree contribute no information and are discarded. This is an extension of the conditional logit model from \cite{chamberlain_analysis_1980} to the two-sided fixed effects setting.


\subsection{Estimation}
\label{sec:estimation}

With these identification restrictions, we estimate $\beta$ by maximizing the log-likelihood function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
    L(\beta) = \sum_{m=1}^M 1\{\tilde{y}_m=-1\} \times \log F(\tilde{x}_m'\beta) \ + \ 1\{\tilde{y}_m=1\} \times \log (1-F(\tilde{x}_m'\beta)),
\end{equation}
where $F$ is the logistic CDF and the summation runs over all discordant quadruples. For inference, we take into account the dependence between quadruples that share nodes and follow \cite{jochmans_fixedeffect_2019}.

While the Jochmans estimator delivers consistent estimates of $\beta$, it eliminates the fixed effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$ and therefore cannot produce predicted referral probabilities directly. To recover the full index $x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_i + \alpha_j$ needed for marginal-effect and counterfactual calculations, we employ a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, we estimate $\hat{\beta}$ from the quadruple-based conditional logit above. In the second stage, we hold $\hat{\beta}$ fixed and recover the doctor and specialist fixed effects by estimating a logit model with two-way fixed effects and the linear index $x_{ij}'\hat{\beta}$ entered as an offset.

Because referral networks are sparse, many doctor-specialist fixed effects are not separately identified in the second stage. We verify the reliability of this procedure in three ways, with full details in the supplemental appendix. First, we confirm that the estimated marginal effects are stable across a wide range of iteration counts (50 to 250), indicating that the non-separated subsample on which we compute marginal effects is not sensitive to the convergence criterion. Second, we compare the non-separated pairs to the full choice set and find that they are similar on observable characteristics, suggesting that the subsample used for marginal-effect calculations is representative. Third, a simulation exercise with a known data-generating process confirms that the two-stage procedure recovers the true parameters and marginal effects.

The supplemental appendix also presents standard conditional logit results conditioning on PCP fixed effects only, as in \citealt{zeltzer2020}.\footnote{The conditional logit with one-sided fixed effects does not require the quadruple construction and can be estimated on the full choice set. Marginal effects from this specification are generally attenuated relative to the two-stage estimates, consistent with omitted specialist heterogeneity biasing the estimates toward zero.}


\subsection{Dynamics}
\label{sec:dynamics-method}

Our mover design also provides a natural framework for studying how referral networks evolve after initial formation. Rather than observing only the first post-move year, we estimate the network formation model separately on expanding post-move windows. The one-year window captures the initial network; the two-year window includes both the initial and second-year referrals, and so on through four years. Comparing the estimated marginal effects across these windows reveals whether the factors driving referral formation persist, strengthen, or attenuate as PCPs accumulate local experience.

A potential concern with cumulative windows is compositional dilution. As the window widens, the denominator of referral links grows, and concordance rates on any given attribute may decline mechanically even if the PCP's behavior is unchanged. We address this in two ways. First, we decompose each window's links into persisting ties (present since the first year) and newly formed ties, and examine whether the new links differ systematically from the persisting ones. Second, we estimate a period-specific specification in which the model is estimated on referrals made in each individual post-move year (rather than cumulative windows), providing a complementary check on whether attenuation reflects genuine behavioral change.

Our dynamics analysis characterizes how the determinants of referral formation change over time but does not identify the specific mechanisms driving that change. For example, PCPs may update their referral networks as they learn about specialist quality from patient outcomes, as studied in \cite{mccarthy2026}. Distinguishing among such mechanisms is beyond the scope of this paper.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

Our analysis relies on the 100\% Medicare fee-for-service (FFS) claims files from 2009–2018. Referrals are constructed from Medicare Part A (inpatient) and Part B (carrier) files. Data on physician characteristics come from CMS Physician Compare, the Medicare Data on Provider Practice and Specialty (MD-PPAS), and the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES). Demographic data from Physician Compare are first available in 2012; however, we rely on those data for time-invariant physician characteristics such as year of medical school graduation.\footnote{CMS Physician Compare also includes data on which medical school a physician attended; however, these data are sparse. Because our quadruple-based estimator requires each variable to be observed for all four physicians in a quadruple, even moderate missingness in the raw data translates to substantial sample loss in the estimation sample, and we therefore exclude medical school from our analysis.} As such, our data construction is relatively unaffected by the late arrival of Physician Compare data.

We study referral formation across three specialty areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. These specialties span a range of clinical settings and referral processes. Orthopedic referrals are identified through inpatient admissions for planned and elective major joint replacement (Diagnosis Related Group codes), while cardiology and dermatology referrals are identified through new-patient evaluation and management (E\&M) office visits (Current Procedural Terminology codes). The three specialties also differ markedly in their demographic composition and organizational structure. Orthopedic surgeons are nearly all male (98\%), cardiologists are predominantly male (88\%), and dermatologists are roughly gender-balanced (56\% male). In the MD-PPAS data, 3.5\% of practice groups containing a PCP also include a cardiologist, 2.8\% include an orthopedic surgeon, and only 1.2\% include a dermatologist, reflecting the tendency of dermatologists to practice independently or in single-specialty groups.

Across all three specialties, we infer the referring PCP as the primary care physician with the highest number of office-based E\&M visits in the twelve months before the specialist encounter, following \cite{pham2009} and \cite{agha2017}, provided the patient saw that PCP at least twice.\footnote{An alternative approach uses the referring physician NPI (\texttt{rfr\_physn\_npi}) recorded on the specialist's claim. For orthopedic surgery, where we can assess this directly, this field is not populated in roughly 32\% of cases, and when populated, it most often lists the surgeon themselves (about 43\%) or a non-PCP specialist (about 7\%), with only 18\% listing a PCP. Our carrier-lookback method identifies a PCP for approximately 80\% of orthopedic procedures and, among cases where both methods identify a PCP, concordance exceeds 82\% in every year.} We limit the sample to PCPs and specialists each with at least 10 encounters over the full 2009–2018 period. Specialty-specific sample construction and summary statistics are discussed in Sections \ref{sec:ortho}--\ref{sec:derm}.

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:model}, our primary variables of interest consist of physician demographics and practice characteristics. Since our analysis ultimately conditions on both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our key practice characteristic is geographic proximity, measured as the distance between zipcode centroids of the PCP’s primary practice location and that of the specialist. These data are available from MD-PPAS. We construct the differential age as well as years since medical school graduation as a measure of similarity in medical experience. These data come from CMS Physician Compare. We also construct measures of gender homophily, again from data in MD-PPAS.

Because physician race is not publicly available on a national scale, we impute physicians’ race by combining three complementary classifiers. First, we obtain name-based probabilities from NamePrism and from the WRU Bayesian surname-geocoding algorithm; second, we scrape profile photos from Zocdoc and use the DeepFace network to generate image-based probabilities. After aligning category labels across methods, we aggregate the separate predictions with a random-forest ensemble. A ``Full’’ model (using all three sources) attains 89\% accuracy for approximately 58,000 physicians, while a ``Name-based’’ model (names only) reaches 82\% accuracy for nearly 1 million physicians. Accuracy is assessed against verified race information drawn from Florida voter files, Texas medical-license records, and self-reported Zocdoc profiles. Full implementation details and accuracy are discussed in the supplemental appendix.

We flag a PCP as a \textit{mover} in the first year they practice in a new Hospital Referral Region (HRR). For every calendar year, we build a two-year window $\{t-1,t\}$, extract each doctor’s HRR in the MD-PPAS panel, and keep those whose HRR in year $t-1$ differs from the HRR in year $t$. To exclude cosmetic boundary shifts, we remove cases in which the origin and destination HRRs are geographic neighbors, identified from the Dartmouth Atlas HRR shapefile and its contiguity matrix. We further drop PCPs who register more than one HRR change inside the window, so every retained observation reflects a single, non-contiguous relocation. A PCP who relocates more than once across the panel (e.g., in 2012 and again in 2016) may appear in multiple annual cohorts, but the quadruples used for estimation are formed within a single HRR-year cell, so repeat movers do not introduce mechanical dependence across cohorts. For our network formation model and subsequent estimation, a PCP is assumed to construct their referral network as a subset of all possible specialists in their HRR. Referral counts among any given PCP-specialist pair are on average small. As such, we define PCP-specialist links as any observed referral between a given PCP and specialist, effectively ignoring the intensive margin.

\section{Orthopedic Surgery}
\label{sec:ortho}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Major joint replacement is among the most common inpatient surgical procedures for Medicare beneficiaries. Because these procedures are planned and elective, the PCP’s referral to an orthopedic surgeon typically occurs well in advance of the surgery, making the referral decision relatively deliberate. Orthopedic surgery is also one of the most male-dominated specialties, with 98\% of surgeons in our data being male. Among practice groups that contain at least one PCP, 2.8\% also include an orthopedic surgeon.

From the Medicare FFS claims data, we identify eligible planned and elective orthopedic surgeries by DRG codes 461, 462, 469, 470, 480--489, and 507--511. We keep only cases in which the operation is performed by an orthopedic surgeon (per MD-PPAS specialty codes) and a referring PCP can be inferred. Our full referral data consist of approximately 566,000 PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 64,000 unique PCPs, and around 15,200 unique specialists. From 2010--2018, we identify 1,234 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-ortho} summarizes the orthopedic referral data, with Panel A presenting information on PCPs and Panel B on specialists. The median PCP refers to 2 distinct orthopedic surgeons, is 53 years old, and is male in 75\% of cases. Movers are younger (48 years), less male (66\%), and refer to fewer specialists immediately after relocating. From Panel B, specialists receive referrals from a median of 6 unique PCPs. Specialists receiving referrals from PCP movers are more likely to be white (92\% versus 90\%) and slightly more likely to be male (97.8\% versus 97.6\%) compared to the full data, despite PCP movers being less likely to be white males than the broader PCP population.

Figure \ref{fig:map-movers-ortho} presents the origin and destination HRRs for PCP movers in the orthopedic sample. The observed referral network is relatively sparse, with links representing 3.5\% of all potential PCP-specialist pairs within each HRR-year cell.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-ortho} contrasts observable concordance between established and non-established referral links. Organizational proximity and geography dominate: 18.9\% of linked pairs share a practice group (versus 2.8\% of non-links), and the average distance is 10 miles for established links versus 42 miles for non-links. Demographic concordance differs modestly between groups, with gender concordance at 66.6\% versus 65.4\% and race concordance at 75.5\% versus 71.4\%.

\subsection{Initial Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-ortho} summarizes the two-stage estimation results for orthopedic surgery. Columns (1)--(3) report the structural coefficients (log-odds) from the Jochmans estimator across three specifications, while columns (4)--(6) present the corresponding average marginal effects. We focus on the marginal effects.

Practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral choice. Sharing a practice group raises the referral probability by approximately 39 percentage points (95\% CI: 22 to 55), dwarfing all other characteristics. Roughly 37\% of PCP movers have at least one same-practice orthopedic surgeon in their choice set, and for those who do, referring within-practice may reflect organizational convenience, such as shared information systems and physical proximity within the practice. Because practice affiliation is so dominant, one natural question is whether the remaining covariates carry any independent explanatory power once within-practice referrals are removed. The supplemental appendix presents a robustness specification that drops practice affiliation entirely, isolating the ``reaching outside the practice'' decision. In this specification, distance and gender concordance retain their signs and significance, confirming that these factors operate independently of organizational co-location.

Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 5 percentage points per 5 miles. Gender concordance increases the probability by around 6 percentage points, a notable finding given that 98\% of orthopedic surgeons are male and gender variation in the specialist pool is limited. Race concordance has a point estimate of 7 percentage points but is imprecisely estimated. Age and experience concordance are economically negligible.

\subsection{Dynamics in Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-ortho} documents how PCP referral networks evolve as we widen the post-move window from one to four years. The average network expands from 1.7 distinct specialists in the move year to 3.9 by year 4. The share of within-practice referral links declines from 18.4\% to 14.7\%, and mean distance increases from 10.5 to 12.7 miles, suggesting that PCPs gradually reach beyond their immediate organizational environment.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-ortho} presents the estimated marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation dominates immediately after relocation but declines monotonically, falling from roughly 39 percentage points in the move year to approximately 15 percentage points by year 4. Gender concordance also dissipates over time, while geographic distance retains a persistent penalty throughout.

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:dynamics-method}, a potential concern with cumulative windows is that attenuation may reflect compositional dilution rather than genuine behavioral change. We address this in two ways. First, the supplemental appendix decomposes each window's links into persisting and newly formed ties. If attenuation were purely mechanical, new links in later windows would show concordance rates similar to those of persisting links. Instead, newly formed ties exhibit progressively lower organizational and demographic concordance. Second, the supplemental appendix presents period-specific (non-cumulative) estimates in which the model is estimated separately on referrals made in each individual post-move year. These estimates show a similar pattern of attenuation, providing further evidence that the decline reflects genuine behavioral change.


\section{Cardiology}
\label{sec:cardio}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Cardiology referrals in our data are based on new-patient E\&M office visits (CPT codes 99201--99205) performed by cardiologists and clinical cardiac electrophysiologists. Unlike orthopedic surgery, these are initial consultations for evaluation rather than discrete surgical procedures, ranging from routine assessments (e.g., lipid management, hypertension follow-up) to more urgent evaluations (e.g., chest pain, arrhythmia workup). We focus on E\&M visits rather than cardiac procedures because the PCP-to-surgeon referral chain for cardiology procedures is less direct, with PCPs typically referring to a general cardiologist who may then refer onward for procedural care. Cardiology is predominantly male (88\% of specialists), though less so than orthopedic surgery. Among PCP-containing practice groups, 3.5\% also include a cardiologist, the highest co-location rate of the three specialties we study, reflecting the integration of cardiology into hospital-based and multispecialty group practices.

We identify 1.67 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 113,000 unique PCPs, and around 23,000 unique specialists. The larger sample reflects the broader volume of cardiology E\&M visits relative to orthopedic surgeries. From 2010--2018, we identify 5,071 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-cardioem} summarizes the cardiology referral data. PCPs refer to a median of 3 distinct cardiologists, compared to 2 for orthopedic surgery, consistent with the higher frequency of cardiology consultations. Movers are younger (46 years) and less male (59\%) than the overall PCP population.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-cardioem} contrasts established and non-established referral links. Same-practice rates are notably higher than in orthopedic surgery: 24.8\% of linked pairs share a practice group (versus 3.4\% of non-links). Distance patterns are similar, with established links averaging 11 miles versus 33 miles for non-links. Demographic concordance is lower overall than in orthopedic surgery, reflecting the more diverse composition of both PCPs and cardiologists in this sample.

\subsection{Initial Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-cardioem} presents the two-stage estimation results. Practice affiliation remains the dominant predictor, but the marginal effect is considerably smaller than in orthopedic surgery at approximately 15 percentage points. This is notable given that cardiology has the highest co-location rate of the three specialties (24.8\% of linked pairs share a practice group), suggesting that the prevalence of same-practice ties does not mechanically determine the magnitude of the practice affiliation effect. Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 2 percentage points per 5 miles, a smaller penalty than in orthopedic surgery, perhaps reflecting the less geographically concentrated nature of cardiology office visits relative to inpatient surgical procedures.

Gender and race concordance are both negligible, with point estimates near zero. With 88\% of cardiologists being male, there is limited variation in the specialist pool for gender concordance to operate on. As with orthopedic surgery, age and experience differences play no meaningful role.

\subsection{Dynamics in Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-cardioem} documents the evolution of cardiology referral networks. Networks expand more rapidly than in orthopedic surgery, from 2.4 specialists in the move year to 6.2 by year 4, reflecting the higher frequency of cardiology consultations. The share of within-practice referral links declines modestly from 24.8\% to 22.1\%, a smaller absolute decline than in orthopedic surgery.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-cardioem} presents the marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation attenuates over time, though the decline is less pronounced than in orthopedic surgery, reflecting the stronger organizational integration of cardiology into multispecialty practice groups. Gender and race concordance remain near zero throughout. The link decomposition in the appendix confirms that this attenuation is not driven by compositional dilution.


\section{Dermatology}
\label{sec:derm}

\subsection{Data and Institutional Context}

Dermatology referrals are based on new-patient E\&M office visits, the same CPT codes used for cardiology. The majority of dermatology referrals are for relatively lower-acuity conditions such as skin checks, rashes, and acne, though skin cancer screening and evaluation can carry higher clinical stakes. Dermatology is the most gender-balanced specialty in our analysis, with 56\% male specialists, and dermatologists tend to practice independently or in single-specialty groups. Only 1.2\% of PCP-containing practice groups include a dermatologist, the lowest co-location rate of the three specialties. This organizational separation makes dermatology a useful contrast to cardiology, where multispecialty group integration is more common.

From 2010--2018, we identify approximately 1.60 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 116,000 unique PCPs, around 13,000 unique specialists, and 4,664 PCP movers. The larger number of movers relative to orthopedic surgery reflects the broader volume of dermatology E\&M visits and the larger PCP pool with at least one dermatology referral.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}

Table \ref{tab:desc-derm} summarizes the dermatology referral data. PCPs refer to a median of 3 distinct dermatologists, and movers are younger (46 years) and less male (56\%) than the overall PCP population. Dermatologists have the highest median in-degree of the three specialties (27 referring PCPs), consistent with fewer dermatologists serving larger catchment areas.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats-derm} contrasts established and non-established links. Same-practice rates are the lowest of the three specialties, at 12.2\% of linked pairs versus 2.0\% of non-links, as expected given the low co-location rate. Distance patterns are similar to the other specialties, with established links averaging 10 miles versus 29 miles for non-links.

\subsection{Initial Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe-derm} presents the two-stage estimation results. Practice affiliation is again the dominant predictor, with a marginal effect of approximately 32 percentage points. Despite dermatology having the lowest co-location rate among the three specialties, the practice affiliation effect is larger than in cardiology and closer to orthopedic surgery. This further illustrates that the marginal effect of practice affiliation does not simply mirror co-location prevalence. Distance lowers the referral probability by roughly 3 percentage points per 5 miles.

Gender concordance is small but positive at approximately 1 percentage point, and race concordance is similarly small at less than 1 percentage point. The small gender effect is notable in light of dermatology being the most gender-balanced specialty in our analysis. If gender concordance were primarily a function of available variation in the specialist pool, one might expect the largest effects where specialists are most gender-diverse. Instead, the largest gender effect appears in orthopedic surgery, where nearly all specialists are male.

\subsection{Dynamics in Network Formation}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics-derm} documents the evolution of dermatology referral networks. Networks expand from 2.3 specialists in the move year to 5.9 by year 4. The share of within-practice referral links declines from 12.1\% to 10.3\%.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-derm} presents the marginal effects by window. Practice affiliation attenuates over time, declining from roughly 32 percentage points to approximately 20 by year 4. The rate of decline is similar to that observed in orthopedic surgery, despite the very different institutional settings. Gender and race concordance remain small throughout. As with the other specialties, the link decomposition in the supplemental appendix confirms that this attenuation is not driven by compositional dilution.


%%% CROSS-SPECIALTY COMPARISON

\section{Cross-Specialty Comparison}
\label{sec:cross-specialty}

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-cross} presents a forest plot of the full-specification marginal effects from Tables \ref{tab:logit-twfe-ortho}--\ref{tab:logit-twfe-derm}. Several patterns emerge.

First, practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral formation in all three specialties, but the magnitude varies substantially. The marginal effect is largest in orthopedic surgery (39 percentage points), followed by dermatology (32) and cardiology (15). This ordering does not simply mirror co-location rates, as cardiology has the highest share of same-practice links (24.8\%) yet the smallest marginal effect, while orthopedic surgery has an intermediate co-location rate (18.9\%) and the largest effect. How organizational ties translate into referral decisions appears to depend on institutional features that differ across specialties, such as the nature of the referral (surgical vs.\ consultative), the degree of integration into multispecialty groups, and the clinical urgency of the referral.

Second, geographic proximity is a robust and persistent predictor across all three specialties, with marginal effects ranging from 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles.

Third, the role of demographic concordance differs across specialties. Gender concordance is largest in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points), small in dermatology (1 percentage point), and negligible in cardiology. This pattern does not scale with the degree of gender variation in the specialist pool. Orthopedic surgery, where specialists are nearly all male, shows the largest gender effect, while dermatology, with a roughly gender-balanced specialist pool, shows a much smaller effect. Race concordance is imprecisely estimated in all three specialties. Age and experience play no meaningful role anywhere.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-cross} presents the dynamics of marginal effects across specialties. The attenuation of practice affiliation over the first four post-move years is the most consistent finding across specialties. The effect declines in all three, with the largest absolute decline in orthopedic surgery. This pattern is consistent with organizational convenience playing a diminishing role as PCPs accumulate local experience and develop referral relationships outside their immediate practice environment.


%%% MECHANISMS

\section{Peer Referrals and Practice Affiliation}
\label{sec:mechanisms}

Practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral formation across all three specialties, but this finding leaves open the question of how organizational ties translate into referral decisions. One possibility is that shared practice membership directly constrains referral choices through internal policies, shared electronic health records, or co-located offices. Another is that newly arrived PCPs learn about local specialists from their practice colleagues and adopt similar referral patterns. To shed light on this distinction, we construct a measure of peer referral behavior and examine whether it accounts for part of the practice affiliation effect.

For each mover, we measure the pre-arrival referral patterns of incumbent PCPs in the mover's new practice. Specifically, for each specialist in the mover's choice set, we count how many other PCPs in the same practice group referred to that specialist in the year before the mover arrived. This lagged measure captures the practice's established referral network at the time the mover enters, avoiding contamination from the mover's own referral decisions.

Table~\ref{tab:peer-ortho} presents the results for orthopedic surgery using a conditional logit specification with PCP fixed effects. Column (1) reproduces the baseline specification without peer referrals. Column (2) adds the peer referral measure alongside practice affiliation. The marginal effect of practice affiliation drops from 6.6 percentage points to 3.2 percentage points, a decline of roughly half. The peer referral variable is positive and significant, with each additional referring peer raising the referral probability by approximately 0.5 percentage points. Column (3) drops practice affiliation entirely and shows that the peer referral coefficient is largely unchanged, suggesting that the two variables capture overlapping but not identical variation.

Tables~\ref{tab:peer-cardioem} and \ref{tab:peer-derm} present analogous results for cardiology and dermatology. The pattern is consistent across all three specialties. Adding peer referrals reduces the practice affiliation effect by roughly half in cardiology (from 7.5 to 2.6 percentage points) and by nearly 80\% in dermatology (from 10.5 to 2.2 percentage points).

These results suggest that a substantial share of the practice affiliation effect operates through the referral behavior of incumbent colleagues. However, we note that peer referral patterns and organizational structure are not easily separable. If a practice has policies or norms that channel referrals to specific specialists, then all PCPs in the practice will exhibit similar referral patterns, and the peer referral variable will absorb what is fundamentally an organizational effect. The residual practice affiliation coefficient in column (2) captures the portion of organizational influence that operates beyond peer referral patterns, but the peer referral coefficient itself reflects a combination of information transmission and shared organizational channeling.


%%% WELFARE

\section{Quality Reallocation}
\label{sec:welfare}

Our results indicate that practice affiliation and geographic proximity are the dominant drivers of referral formation, raising a natural question for policy: do these convenience-driven referral patterns come at a cost to patient outcomes? If PCPs are choosing specialists based on organizational ties rather than clinical quality, the welfare implications could be substantial. To gauge the magnitude of any such quality loss, we turn to a counterfactual exercise.

Orthopedic surgery referrals culminate in discrete surgical episodes (major joint replacements) for which well-defined outcome metrics exist, most notably readmission rates.\footnote{We measure surgeon quality using 90-day readmission rates following major joint replacement, drawing on quality measures developed under the CMS Comprehensive Care for Joint Replacement Model and the National Quality Forum.} Cardiology and dermatology referrals, by contrast, typically lead to office-based consultations for evaluation rather than discrete procedures, making quality measurement substantially more difficult. We therefore restrict the quality analysis to orthopedic surgery, not because quality data are unavailable for the other specialties per se, but because the clinical setting permits a more credible and interpretable quality measure.

For orthopedic surgery, we assess the quality implications of referral preferences by computing counterfactual referral probabilities under which the coefficient on each characteristic is set to zero. These exercises, presented in the supplemental appendix, suggest that the quality losses attributable to organizational and demographic matching are modest, consistent with the relatively small marginal effects for most covariates outside of practice affiliation.


%%% DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This paper examines the determinants of initial referral networks between PCPs and specialists and how those networks evolve over time. We study three clinical areas with distinct institutional settings, namely orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology, using the 100\% Medicare FFS claims data from 2009 through 2018. Our identification strategy exploits PCP movers who relocate to a new market and must build their referral networks from scratch. We employ the quadruple-based estimator of \cite{jochmans2018}, which allows for both PCP and specialist fixed effects, together with a second-stage fixed-effect recovery procedure that enables marginal-effect calculations on the probability scale.

Across all three specialties, practice group affiliation is by far the largest contributor to initial link formation, with geographic proximity also emerging as a robust and persistent factor. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery but plays a smaller role in dermatology and is negligible for cardiology. Other measures of demographic concordance, including age, experience, and race, play at best a limited role. As PCPs spend more time in their new market, the effects of practice affiliation attenuate in all three specialties. Link decomposition and period-specific estimation confirm that this decline reflects genuine behavioral change rather than compositional dilution, consistent with organizational convenience playing a diminishing role as PCPs accumulate local information.

While we employ a richer econometric specification than \cite{zeltzer2020}, allowing for both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our baseline results for practice affiliation and gender concordance in orthopedic surgery are broadly consistent with that work, albeit with somewhat different magnitudes. By examining three specialties with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, we show that the dominance of organizational ties in referral formation is not specific to any single clinical area, though the magnitude varies across specialties.

We conclude with three policy observations. First, while increasing horizontal integration of physician practices can have large negative consequences with regard to competitiveness and physician pricing \citep{koch2021}, our results suggest that, insomuch as mergers alter physician referrals, the quality implications of such referral changes are likely minor. Second, our results highlight the role of geographic proximity as a persistent factor in referral formation. If preferences for demographic homophily were extended to the entire pool of specialists in an HRR, the quality of care consequences could be meaningful; however, when referral choices are constrained to a narrow geographic subset of specialists, such preferences have less opportunity to negatively affect patient care. Third, while organizational affiliation plays a dominant role in PCPs’ initial referral networks, these effects attenuate over time across all three specialties, suggesting that the initial convenience-driven network is not permanent and that PCPs do adjust their referral patterns as they gain local experience.
