\begin{abstract}
\noindent With one-third of U.S. primary care visits generating a specialist referral, primary care physicians (PCPs) have outsized influence over how patients are allocated across the specialist market and the subsequent care that patients receive. Focusing on PCPs who enter or relocate to a new market, we study the determinants of initial PCP–specialist networks using the universe of 2010–2018 Medicare fee‑for‑service claims across three distinct specialty areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. A network‑formation model with two‑sided fixed effects shows that sharing a common practice is the dominant factor in forming initial referral links across all three specialties, followed by geographic proximity and relatively minor effects from homophily across various demographic characteristics. The role of organizational affiliation dissipates over the first four post‑move years, consistent with newly arrived physicians relying on convenience and familiarity when local information is limited and gradually shifting away from such factors over time.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Expert referrals shape the allocation of high‐stakes services in many industries, including finance, law, and health care; however, these referrals are often made in the face of significant uncertainty, where one expert may not be fully informed about the quality and skills of other experts in the market. In such settings, access to higher quality services can be facilitated or denied implicitly due to the existing network of the referring experts. Understanding decision making in these contexts remains an important area of research across several disciplines \citep{ching2013}. 

One area where decision making under uncertainty is particularly salient is in referrals from primary care physicians (PCPs) to specialists. When a PCP refers a patient to a specialist, that decision is made under potentially severe information frictions, and yet the referral matters significantly as it fixes the locus of subsequent treatment. The U.S. health care system is increasingly reliant on such referrals, with as many as 30\% of visits to PCPs resulting in a referral to a specialist for additional care \citep{wright2022}, a near two-fold increase in referral rates over the preceding decade \citep{barnett2012aim, mehrotra2011}, and a heavy reliance on physician recommendations among patients \citep{chernew2021}. The initial referral of PCPs to specialists is therefore a critical step in the health care process, shaping both patient outcomes and the allocation of patients across the specialist market.

This paper provides some of the first causal evidence on the determinants of initial PCP–specialist referral networks. To do so, we focus on referrals from PCPs who locate or relocate to a new geographic market and arrive with no previously observed local referral ties, therefore constructing or reconstructing their networks from scratch.\footnote{This mover strategy requires that PCPs do not relocate specifically because of the market's set of available specialist referrals, which is a plausible assumption in our setting and supported in our data (see Tables~\ref{tab:desc-doctors} and \ref{tab:desc-specialists}).} Using 100\% Medicare fee‐for‐service (FFS) claims from 2010--2018, we study referral formation across three specialty areas that span a range of clinical settings and physician demographics, namely orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. For each specialty, we estimate a network‐formation model with two-sided fixed effects for both the referring PCP and the specialist. Standard conditional logit estimation of such models is inconsistent due to the incidental parameters problem, so we adopt the quadruple-based estimator of \cite{jochmans2018}, which differences out both sets of fixed effects. We then recover the fixed effects in a second stage in order to compute marginal effects on the probability scale. A simulation exercise, presented in the supplemental appendix, confirms that this two-stage procedure can recover the true coefficient estimates and marginal effects, offering a substantial improvement to the analogous one-sided fixed effect estimates and marginal effects via standard conditional logit.

% Results: Initial formation
Across all three specialties, practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of initial referral formation. The effect is largest in orthopedic surgery, where sharing a practice group raises the referral probability by roughly 39 percentage points (95\% CI: 22 to 55), followed by dermatology (32 percentage points) and cardiology (15 percentage points). Geographic proximity consistently lowers the referral probability by 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points) and small but positive in dermatology (1 percentage point), while negligible for cardiology. Race, age, and experience concordance have economically small effects across all specialties.

% Results: Dynamics
We also study how these referral networks evolve as PCPs spend more time in their new market. Estimating our network formation model separately over expanding post-move windows (one year, two years, etc.), we find that the effect of practice affiliation attenuates over four post-move years in all three specialties. Gender concordance also dissipates over time in orthopedic surgery, while geographic proximity remains a persistent factor.\footnote{These results persist whether we consider cumulative referral networks over time or focus only on newly formed links, indicating genuine behavioral change rather than mechanical dilution or regression to the mean.} We further decompose the practice affiliation effect and find that a large portion of the effect appears to be from mirroring referral networks of a PCP's new peers, suggesting information transmission within practices as a key mechanism. Ultimately, our examination of the dynamics of referral networks shows that newly arrived physicians rely heavily on organizational convenience and peer influence when local information is limited and gradually shift away from such factors over time. 

% Results: Welfare
For orthopedic surgery, where discrete surgical episodes allow for well-defined quality measurement, we gauge the quality implications of these referral preferences by computing counterfactual referral probabilities under which the coefficient on each characteristic is set to zero. We measure surgeon quality from a combination of mortality, readmissions, and complications, following the CMS Comprehensive Care for Joint Replacement Model and the National Quality Forum. We then calculate the change in referral probability multiplied by the quality differential per link, and we average within HRR markets. The resulting metric shows how much each covariate pushes referrals toward or away from higher-quality specialists, where we find that the quality losses attributable to organizational and demographic matching are modest at most.

% Contribution 1: Referral networks
Our analysis contributes to three distinct areas of economics and health policy. First, we contribute to the literature on physician referral networks. This literature typically considers physician networks in the context of shared patients \citep{landon2012, barnett2012mc, landon2018, linde2019}, wherein authors examine social networks of physicians defined as the set of physicians that see the same patients within a designated time period. These studies tend to envision an ``undirected'' physician network, in that the edges (i.e., connections between two physicians) reflect a two-way relationship with patients flowing from one physician to another in both directions. Our analysis more closely aligns with a smaller literature focusing on ``directed'' graphs in which referring physicians such as PCPs are connected to specialists \citep{agha2017, agha2018, zeltzer2020}. This literature typically considers physician networks as an input into health care production, while our analysis instead examines network formation itself. 

Our analysis aligns most directly with \cite{zeltzer2020}, who examines gender homophily in specialist referrals. However, our analysis remains distinct from \cite{zeltzer2020} in three key ways: 1) we study referral formation separately within three well-defined specialty areas, each with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, rather than pooling across a mix of conditions; 2) by focusing on PCP movers who must build referral networks from scratch, we can study both initial network formation and the subsequent evolution of referral relationships, separating the forces that govern first contact from those that sustain or reshape ties over time; and 3) taking advantage of recent advances in the econometrics of networks \citep{jochmans2018, jochmans_fixedeffect_2019}, we allow for a richer set of both PCP and specialist unobserved fixed effects (see also \cite{graham_econometric_2017} and \cite{dzemski2019}).

% Contribution 2: Referrals as a physician agency problem
Second, we contribute to the literature on physician agency with regard to referrals. This research extends the traditional role of physician agency to consider the role of physicians not just on the quantity and type of health care used, but also on the location of care \citep{baker2016, walden2016, lin2025}. In the context of PCPs, the physician's decision-making authority is most salient in the referral process. Indeed, \cite{freedman2015} find that the PCP's recommendation is the most commonly cited reason from a patient in their selection of an oncologist, with similar results documented in \cite{barkowski2018} and \cite{chernew2021}. Like these studies, we consider PCP referrals as an important dimension of physician agency; however, rather than taking the PCP's referral network as given, our analysis seeks to determine how such networks are initially formed and determinants of networks change over time.

% Contribution 3: Understanding sources of supply-side variation in health care
Finally, we contribute to the broader study of sources of supply-side variation in health care. Overall, hospital and physician services constitute the two largest components of U.S. health expenditures and jointly accounted for nearly \$2 trillion in U.S. health spending in 2019 (52\% of total health expenditures); however, these expenditures are not distributed uniformly across geographic areas \citep{wennberg1973, gottlieb2010, miller2011, wennberg2003}. Rather, expenditures are characterized by areas of very high health care utilization alongside areas of very low utilization, and there is strong empirical evidence that the majority of this geographic variation is not driven by patient preferences \citep{zuckerman2010,finkelstein2016,badinski2023} or by differences in quality of care \citep{skinner1997,baicker2004ha}. Authors estimate that as much as 60\% of residual geographic variation in health care expenditures can be explained by provider behaviors as opposed to patient health care needs \citep{finkelstein2016}, and among this, physician practice style is the dominant factor \citep{molitor2018, badinski2023}. There is also substantial variation in quality and costs across physicians within the same geographic area \citep{cooper2019, epstein2009, moy2020}. 

The conclusion from this literature is that a large share of otherwise unexplained variation in health care expenditures and quality of care is driven by provider behaviors and facilitated by physicians’ underlying influence on treatment decisions and location of care. Policy solutions to remove this variation nonetheless remain elusive and require significant changes in physician behaviors \citep{wilensky2016}. Our analysis considers referral patterns from PCPs to specialists as an important potential contributor to these existing inefficiencies. Given the PCP’s role as a patient navigator, referral networks are a natural candidate for examining sources of and potential solutions to such variation. If PCPs can better direct patients to more efficient and higher quality specialists, there is ample opportunity to improve patient health and lower health care costs. Moreover, because redirecting patients through better-informed referrals is typically easier than altering specialists' established practice styles, refining referral networks may offer a relatively low-cost lever for reducing spending and improving outcomes.


\section{PCP Referral Networks}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{A Model of Network Formation}
Our analysis follows a conceptual model in which a PCP's decision to initiate a referral relationship with a specialist is a function of three broad factors: 1) similarity in observable characteristics between the PCP and the specialist, denoted by $x_{ij}$; 2) the utility value of unobserved characteristics, denoted $\alpha_{ij}$; and 3) idiosyncratic shocks that affect the formation of a referral link, denoted by $\varepsilon_{ij}$. Note that this setup considers the network formation decision of a PCP, not a given referral, and the outcome of interest is ultimately whether a PCP has any referral relationship with a given specialist. With this setup, a referral link between PCP $i$ and specialist $j$ will exist if the latent utility of such a relationship is positive:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:referral}
    y_{ij} = \mathbf{1} \left(x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_{ij} - \varepsilon_{ij} \geq 0\right),
\end{equation}
where $y_{ij}$ takes the value of 1 if PCP $i$ refers to specialist $j$ and 0 otherwise. In our application, the vector of observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, captures several measures of practice and demographic homophily, including working in the same practice group, differential years of experience, and homophily in gender and race. Geographic proximity also enters the model through $x_{ij}$, which we measure as the distance between PCP and specialist practice locations.

We parameterize the unobserved component as $\alpha_{ij} = \alpha_{i} + \alpha_{j}$, where $\alpha_{i}$ and $\alpha_{j}$ are individual-specific effects for PCPs and specialists, respectively. The PCP effect $\alpha_{i}$ reflects, for example, a PCP's productivity or propensity to refer, and $\alpha_{j}$ similarly captures specialists' time-invariant prestige or quality (over the relative short run). In principle, physicians who practice in the same geographic area may also share unobserved preferences that make a referral link more likely, independent of their observable characteristics. We address this sorting concern through our mover-based research design rather than through additional model parameters, as discussed in Section \ref{sec:identification}.

Lastly, the error term, $\varepsilon_{ij}$, captures idiosyncratic shocks that affect the formation of a referral link. Under the assumption that $\varepsilon_{ij}$ follows a Type I extreme value distribution, the probability of observing a referral relationship from PCP $i$ to specialist $j$ takes the logistic form,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:probability}
    \operatorname{Pr}(y_{ij} = 1 | x_{ij}) = \frac{e^{x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_{ij}}}{1 + e^{x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_{ij}}}.
\end{equation}
Equations \eqref{eq:referral} and \eqref{eq:probability} therefore capture a reduced-form analog to a network formation model that accounts for unobserved heterogeneity of doctors and specialists. 

Link formation based on observed characteristics, $x_{ij}$, and unobserved heterogeneity, $\alpha_{ij}$, describe two distinct features of real world networks, namely sorting on observables (including homophily, distance, etc.) and degree heterogeneity. These modeling choices mirror a large empirical literature on homophily and degree heterogeneity in physician networks. Homophilous matching on practice environment and personal traits has been documented for patient–sharing ties \citep{barnett2012mc,landon_using_2013} and for gender–concordant referrals \citep{zeltzer2020,sarsons2023}. Degree dispersion, captured here by the PCP- and specialist-specific effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$, also follows that of recent network-formation models such as \cite{jackson2007}, \cite{graham_econometric_2017}, and \cite{dzemski2019}. The key parameter of interest in Equation \ref{eq:probability} is $\beta$, which captures the effect of observed characteristics on the referral formation.


\subsection{Identification}
\label{sec:identification}

Our identification strategy rests on two design choices. The first is a focus on PCP ``movers,'' physicians who relocate to a non-contiguous Hospital Referral Region (HRR) and therefore arrive in a new market with no previously observed local referral ties. Established PCP-specialist links embed years of accumulated learning and habit formation, so an analysis that pools all referrals may conflate initial link formation with the subsequent evolution of relationships, ultimately introducing potential bias in our estimate of $\beta$. Focusing on physicians at the moment they enter a new HRR lets us observe referral choices made before such path dependence arises and better isolates the forces that govern where and how initial links form. 

Under the assumption that PCPs do not relocate to a specific HRR in order to refer to particular specialists, this design ultimately helps to eliminate correlation between unobserved PCP and specialist characteristics that arises from shared location preferences. This assumption is plausible if, for example, physician relocations are driven by broader career opportunities, family considerations, or institutional recruitment rather than by the composition of the local specialist pool. A violation would require PCPs to select their destination HRR based on the demographic or practice characteristics of specialists in that market. If such selection were present, it would likely bias our estimates upward, as PCPs who sort into markets with demographically similar specialists would exhibit higher concordance than PCPs who relocate for unrelated reasons. To assess the reasonableness of this assumption, Tables~\ref{tab:desc-doctors} and \ref{tab:desc-specialists} show that movers are broadly similar to the full PCP and specialist samples on race and geographic proximity, with modest differences in age and gender consistent with relocation being more common earlier in a physician's career. This pattern supports the view that moves are not driven by the composition of the destination specialist pool.

The second design choice is the use of discordant quadruples, following \cite{jochmans2018}. Each quadruple $m$ consists of two PCPs $(i, k)$ and two specialists $(j, l)$. We transform the outcome $y_{ij}$ and characteristics $x_{ij}$ as
\begin{align*}
    \tilde{y}_m & = \frac{(y_{ij} - y_{il}) - (y_{kj} - y_{kl})}{2} \text{ and} \\
    \tilde{x}_m & = (x_{ij} - x_{il}) - (x_{kj} - x_{kl}).
\end{align*}
This double-differencing is conceptually analogous to a difference-in-differences estimator. The within-PCP differences, $(y_{ij} - y_{il})$ and $(y_{kj} - y_{kl})$, each remove their respective PCP fixed effect, leaving only the specialist difference $\alpha_j - \alpha_l$. Differencing across the two PCPs then eliminates the specialist fixed effects as well, so that identification of $\beta$ derives entirely from $\tilde{y} \in \{-1, 1\}$ (i.e., from quadruples in which the two PCPs rank the two specialists differently). Quadruples in which the two PCPs agree contribute no information and are discarded. This is an extension of the conditional logit model from \cite{chamberlain_analysis_1980} to the two-sided fixed effects setting. Alternative estimators such as \cite{graham_econometric_2017} and \cite{dzemski2019} jointly estimate the fixed effects and homophily parameters and apply bias corrections in the estimates of the homophily parameters. We instead adopt the quadruple-based approach because it delivers consistent $\widehat{\beta}$ without requiring a bias correction, and because our setting, unlike the network formation model of \cite{dzemski2019}, involves directed links with no meaningful reciprocity.\footnote{The supplemental appendix presents a link-level conditional logit specification with PCP and year fixed effects, analogous to \citet{zeltzer2020}, which retains the full sample and yields qualitatively similar conclusions. The appendix also compares physicians included in the Jochmans discordant quadruples to those excluded, finding broadly similar observable characteristics across the two groups.} 


\subsection{Estimation}
\label{sec:estimation}

Following Section \ref{sec:identification}, we estimate $\beta$ by maximizing the log-likelihood function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:likelihood}
    L(\beta) = \sum_{m=1}^M 1\{\tilde{y}_m=-1\} \times \log \Lambda(\tilde{x}_m'\beta) \ + \ 1\{\tilde{y}_m=1\} \times \log (1-\Lambda(\tilde{x}_m'\beta)),
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda(\cdot)$ is the logistic CDF and the summation runs over all discordant quadruples. We cluster standard errors at the HRR level to account for within-market dependence across quadruples.

While the Jochmans estimator delivers consistent estimates of $\beta$, it also eliminates the fixed effects $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$ and therefore cannot produce predicted referral probabilities directly. To recover the full index $x_{ij}'\beta + \alpha_i + \alpha_j$ needed for marginal effects and quality implications, we employ a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, we estimate $\hat{\beta}$ from the quadruple-based conditional logit estimated from Equation \eqref{eq:likelihood}. In the second stage, we hold $\hat{\beta}$ fixed and recover the PCP and specialist fixed effects by estimating a logit model on the full (non-differenced) data with two-way fixed effects and the linear index $x_{ij}'\hat{\beta}$ entered as an offset. That is, we estimate $\alpha_i$ and $\alpha_j$ from the   model $\operatorname{Pr}(y_{ij}=1) = \Lambda(\alpha_i + \alpha_j + x_{ij}'\hat{\beta})$, where $x_{ij}'\hat{\beta}$ is treated as known. The recovered fixed effects, combined with $\hat{\beta}$, yield predicted referral probabilities for each PCP-specialist pair. Average marginal effects are computed as discrete changes in the predicted probabilities (unit change for binary covariates, 5-mile change for distance) with delta-method standard errors. We validate this two-stage procedure via Monte Carlo simulation in the supplemental appendix, showing that our approach recovers true parameters under varying degrees of FE-covariate correlation.\footnote{The conditional logit with one-sided fixed effects does not require the quadruple construction and can be estimated on the full choice set. Marginal effects from this specification are generally attenuated relative to the two-stage estimates, consistent with omitted specialist heterogeneity biasing the estimates toward zero. Nonetheless, the supplemental appendix also presents standard conditional logit results conditioning on PCP fixed effects only.} 

Because referral networks are sparse, a nontrivial share of specialist fixed effects cannot be identified in the second stage. This is because, to identify a specialist fixed effect $\alpha_j$, we need to observe at least one PCP who refers to specialist $j$ in our sample. With movers making only a handful of referrals each, many specialists appear in PCPs' choice sets but are never referred to by any mover, so their outcomes are all zeros and their fixed effects are not identified. These specialists are dropped, along with every PCP-specialist pair involving them. We refer to the remaining observations as the non-separated subsample and verify its reliability in two ways, with full details in the supplemental appendix. First, we confirm that the estimated marginal effects are stable across a wide range of iteration counts (50 to 250), indicating that the non-separated subsample is not sensitive to the convergence criterion. Second, we compare the non-separated pairs to the full choice set and find that they are similar on observable characteristics, so the reported marginal effects are representative of the full sample.

\subsection{Dynamics}
\label{sec:dynamics-method}

Our mover design also provides a natural framework for studying dynamic effects of referral network formation, separately from the initial network. Rather than observing only the first post-move year, we estimate the network formation model separately on expanding post-move windows. The one-year window captures the initial network, the two-year window includes both the initial and second-year referrals, and so on through four years. Comparing the estimated marginal effects across these windows reveals whether the factors driving referral formation persist, strengthen, or attenuate as PCPs accumulate local experience.

A potential concern with cumulative windows is compositional dilution. As the window widens, the denominator of referral links grows, and concordance rates on any given attribute may decline mechanically even if the PCP's behavior is unchanged. We address this in two ways. First, we decompose each window's links into persisting ties (present since the first year) and newly formed ties, and examine whether the new links differ systematically from the persisting ones. Second, we estimate coefficients separately per post-move year rather than cumulative windows, providing a complementary check on whether attenuation reflects genuine behavioral change.

Our dynamics analysis characterizes how the determinants of referral formation change over time but does not identify the specific mechanisms driving that change. For example, PCPs may update their referral networks as they learn about specialist quality from patient outcomes. Distinguishing among such mechanisms is beyond the scope of this paper but is the subject of ongoing work \citep{mccarthy2026}.

\section{Data Sources and Sample Construction}
\label{sec:data}

Our analysis relies on the 100\% Medicare fee-for-service (FFS) claims files from 2008--2018, in particular the Medicare Part A (inpatient) and Part B (carrier) files. Data on physician characteristics come from CMS Physician Compare, the Medicare Data on Provider Practice and Specialty (MD-PPAS), and the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES). Demographic data from Physician Compare are first available in 2012; however, we rely on those data for time-invariant physician characteristics such as year of medical school graduation.\footnote{CMS Physician Compare also includes data on which medical school a physician attended; however, these data are sparse. Because our quadruple-based estimator requires each variable to be observed for all four physicians in a quadruple, even moderate missingness in the raw data translates to substantial sample loss in the estimation sample, and we therefore exclude medical school from our analysis.} As such, our data construction is relatively unaffected by the late arrival of Physician Compare data.

Across all three specialties, we infer the referring PCP as the primary care physician with the highest number of office-based E\&M visits in the twelve months before the specialist encounter, following \cite{pham2009} and \cite{agha2017}, provided the patient saw that PCP at least twice.\footnote{An alternative approach uses the referring physician NPI (\texttt{rfr\_physn\_npi}) recorded on the specialist's claim, but this can be an unreliable measure of the PCP. For example, in the case of orthopedic surgery, this field on the inpatient claim is not populated in roughly 32\% of cases, and when populated, it most often lists the surgeon themselves (about 43\%) or a non-PCP specialist (about 7\%), with only 18\% listing a PCP. Our carrier-lookback method identifies a PCP for approximately 80\% of orthopedic procedures and, among cases where both methods identify a PCP, concordance exceeds 82\% in every year.} The lookback period for PCP identification along with our mover design effectively consume up to two years of data. Our analysis sample therefore spans 2010--2018. We further limit the sample to PCPs and specialists each with at least 10 encounters over the full 2010--2018 period.

We study referral formation across three specialty areas: orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology. We selected these different specialties so as to consider a range of clinical settings and referral processes. Orthopedic referrals are identified through inpatient admissions for planned and elective major joint replacement via Diagnosis Related Group (DRG) codes, while cardiology and dermatology referrals are identified through new-patient evaluation and management (E\&M) office visits via Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes. The three specialties also differ markedly in their demographic composition and organizational structure. Orthopedic surgeons are nearly all male (98\%), cardiologists are predominantly male but less so than orthopedists (88\%), and dermatologists are roughly gender-balanced (56\% male). In the MD-PPAS data, 3.5\% of practice groups containing a PCP also include a cardiologist, 2.8\% include an orthopedic surgeon, and only 1.2\% include a dermatologist, reflecting the tendency of dermatologists to practice independently or in single-specialty groups.

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:model}, our primary variables of interest consist of physician demographics and practice characteristics. Since our analysis ultimately conditions on both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our key practice characteristic (other than indicators for sharing the same practice) is geographic proximity, measured as the distance between zipcode centroids of the PCP’s primary practice location and that of the specialist. These data are available from MD-PPAS. We construct the differential age as well as years since medical school graduation as a measure of similarity in medical experience. These data come from CMS Physician Compare. We also construct measures of gender homophily, again from data in MD-PPAS.

Because physician race is not publicly available on a national scale, we impute physicians’ race by combining three complementary classifiers. First, we obtain name-based probabilities from NamePrism and from the WRU Bayesian surname-geocoding algorithm. Second, we obtain profile photos from publicly available Zocdoc physician profile pages and use the DeepFace network to generate image-based probabilities. After aligning category labels across methods, we aggregate the separate predictions with a random-forest ensemble. A ``Full’’ model (using all three sources) attains 89\% accuracy for approximately 58,000 physicians, while a ``Name-based’’ model (names only) reaches 82\% accuracy for nearly 1 million physicians. Accuracy is assessed against verified race information drawn from Florida voter files, Texas medical-license records, and self-reported Zocdoc profiles. Full implementation details and accuracy are discussed in the supplemental appendix.

We flag a PCP as a mover in the first year they practice in a new HRR. For every calendar year, we build a two-year window $\{t-1,t\}$, extract each doctor’s HRR in the MD-PPAS panel, and keep those whose HRR in year $t-1$ differs from the HRR in year $t$. To exclude cosmetic boundary shifts, we remove cases in which the origin and destination HRRs are geographic neighbors, identified from the Dartmouth Atlas HRR shapefile and its contiguity matrix. We further drop PCPs who register more than one HRR change inside the window, so every retained observation reflects a single, non-contiguous relocation. A PCP who relocates more than once across the panel (e.g., in 2012 and again in 2016) may appear in multiple annual cohorts, but the quadruples used for estimation are formed within a single HRR-year cell, so repeat movers do not introduce mechanical dependence across cohorts. Figure \ref{fig:movers} maps the origin-destination flows for PCP movers, with each arc connecting a mover's pre- and post-relocation HRR, limited to highest frequency origin and destination HRRs for brevity. The flows span the full geography of the United States, with no dominant corridor or regional concentration. This geographic dispersion is important for our identification strategy, as it suggests that the mover sample is not driven by a small number of local labor market shocks. 

For our network formation model and subsequent estimation, a PCP is assumed to construct their referral network as a subset of all possible specialists in their HRR. Referral counts among any given PCP-specialist pair are on average small. As such, we define PCP-specialist links as any observed referral between a given PCP and specialist, effectively ignoring the intensive margin.

\subsection{Specialty-Specific Sample Construction}

\paragraph{Orthopedic surgery.} Major joint replacement is among the most common inpatient surgical procedures for Medicare beneficiaries. Since we limit to planned and elective inpatient procedures, the PCP's referral to an orthopedic surgeon typically occurs well in advance of the surgery, making the referral decision relatively deliberate.\footnote{While joint replacements can now take place in an outpatient setting, hip and knee replacements remained on the inpatient-only list over essentially the entirety of our data. Total knee replacements, for example, were only reimbursable in an outpatient setting as of January 2018, and hip replacements two years later in 2020.} Orthopedic surgery is also one of the most male-dominated specialties, with 98\% of surgeons in our data being male. Among practice groups that contain at least one PCP, 2.8\% also include an orthopedic surgeon. We identify eligible procedures by DRG codes 461, 462, 469, 470, 480--489, and 507--511, keeping only cases performed by an orthopedic surgeon with an identifiable referring PCP. Our orthopedic sample consists of approximately 566,000 PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 64,000 unique PCPs, around 15,200 unique specialists, and 1,234 PCP movers.

\paragraph{Cardiology.} Cardiology referrals in our data are based on new-patient E\&M office visits (CPT codes 99201--99205) performed by cardiologists and clinical cardiac electrophysiologists. Unlike orthopedic surgery, these are initial consultations for evaluation rather than discrete surgical procedures, ranging from routine assessments (e.g., lipid management, hypertension follow-up) to more urgent evaluations (e.g., chest pain, arrhythmia workup). We focus on E\&M visits rather than cardiac procedures because the PCP-to-surgeon referral chain for cardiology procedures is less direct, with PCPs often referring to a general cardiologist who may then refer onward for procedural care. Cardiology is predominantly male (88\% of specialists), though less so than orthopedic surgery. Among PCP-containing practice groups, 3.5\% also include a cardiologist. This is the highest co-location rate of the three specialties, reflecting the integration of cardiology into hospital-based and multispecialty group practices. Our cardiology sample consists of 1.67 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 113,000 unique PCPs, around 23,000 unique specialists, and 5,071 PCP movers.

\paragraph{Dermatology.} Dermatology referrals are based on the same new-patient E\&M CPT codes used for cardiology. The majority of dermatology referrals are for relatively lower-acuity conditions such as skin checks, rashes, and acne, though skin cancer screening and evaluation can carry higher clinical stakes. Dermatology is also the most gender-balanced specialty in our analysis, with 56\% male specialists, and only 1.2\% of PCP-containing practice groups include a dermatologist, the lowest co-location rate of the three specialties. This organizational separation makes dermatology a useful contrast to cardiology, where multispecialty group integration is more common. Our dermatology sample consists of 1.60 million PCP-specialist pairs, roughly 116,000 unique PCPs, around 13,000 unique specialists, and 4,664 PCP movers.

\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}
\label{sec:desc}

Tables~\ref{tab:desc-doctors} and \ref{tab:desc-specialists} summarize physician and referral characteristics for each specialty. Across all three, PCP movers are younger and less male than the full PCP population, consistent with younger physicians being more geographically mobile. The typical PCP refers to 2--3 distinct specialists, with movers initially referring to fewer specialists immediately after relocating. Specialist characteristics vary with the specialty’s demographic composition. Orthopedic surgeons are nearly all male (98\%), cardiologists are predominantly male (88\%), and dermatologists are roughly gender-balanced (56\% male). Dermatologists have the highest median in-degree (27 referring PCPs), consistent with fewer dermatologists serving larger catchment areas.

Table~\ref{tab:link-stats} contrasts observable concordance between established and non-established referral links across all three specialties. Organizational proximity and geography dominate the contrast in every specialty. Same-practice rates among established links range from 12.2\% in dermatology to 24.8\% in cardiology, compared to 2--3\% among non-links. Average distance is roughly 10--11 miles for established links versus 29--42 miles for non-links. Demographic concordance differs more modestly between established and non-established links, with gender and race concordance gaps of 1--5 percentage points.


%%% RESULTS

\section{Initial Network Formation}
\label{sec:formation}

Table~\ref{tab:logit-twfe} presents the estimation results for all three specialties. Columns (1)--(3) report odds ratios from the full-specification Jochmans estimator, while columns (4)--(6) present the corresponding average marginal effects from our two-stage procedure. Progressive specifications are available in the supplemental appendix. Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-cross} summarizes our results in a forest plot of the full-specification marginal effects across all three specialties. We discuss specific results below, focusing on average marginal for practice affiliation, geographic proximity, and demographic concordance. 

\paragraph{Practice affiliation.} Sharing a practice group is the dominant predictor of referral formation in all three specialties, but the magnitude varies substantially. The marginal effect is largest in orthopedic surgery (39 percentage points), followed by dermatology (32) and cardiology (15). This ordering does not simply mirror co-location rates. Cardiology has the highest share of same-practice links (24.8\%) yet the smallest marginal effect, while orthopedic surgery has an intermediate co-location rate (18.9\%) and the largest effect. How organizational ties translate into referral decisions appears to depend on institutional features that differ across specialties, such as the nature of the referral (surgical vs.\ consultative), the degree of integration into multispecialty groups, and the clinical urgency of the referral. Because practice affiliation is so dominant, the supplemental appendix presents a robustness specification that drops it entirely, isolating the ``reaching outside the practice’’ decision. In that specification, distance and gender concordance retain their signs and significance across all three specialties, confirming that these factors operate independently of organizational co-location.

\paragraph{Geographic proximity.} Distance is a robust and persistent predictor across all three specialties, with marginal effects ranging from 2 to 5 percentage points per five miles. The penalty is largest in orthopedic surgery, perhaps reflecting the geographic concentration of surgical referrals relative to office-based consultations.

\paragraph{Demographic concordance.} Gender concordance is largest in orthopedic surgery (6 percentage points), small in dermatology (1 percentage point), and negligible in cardiology. This pattern does not scale with the degree of gender variation in the specialist pool. Orthopedic surgery, where specialists are nearly all male, shows the largest gender effect, while dermatology, with a roughly gender-balanced specialist pool, shows a much smaller effect. Race concordance is imprecisely estimated in all three specialties. Age and experience play no meaningful role anywhere.

\section{Dynamics in Network Formation}
\label{sec:dynamics}

Table~\ref{tab:dynamics} documents how PCP referral networks evolve as we widen the post-move window from one to four years. Across all three specialties, networks expand substantially: from 1.7 to 3.9 distinct specialists in orthopedic surgery, 2.4 to 6.2 in cardiology, and 2.3 to 5.9 in dermatology. The faster expansion in cardiology and dermatology reflects the higher frequency of E\&M consultations relative to inpatient surgical procedures. In all three specialties, the share of within-practice referral links declines as networks mature, and mean distance increases, suggesting that PCPs gradually reach beyond their immediate organizational environment.

Figure~\ref{fig:mfx-window-cross} presents the estimated marginal effects by referral window across all three specialties. The attenuation of practice affiliation over the first four post-move years is the most consistent finding. The effect declines in all three, with the largest absolute decline in orthopedic surgery (from roughly 39 to 15 percentage points). In cardiology, the decline is less pronounced, reflecting the stronger organizational integration of cardiology into multispecialty practice groups. Gender concordance dissipates over time in orthopedic surgery, while geographic distance retains a persistent penalty across all specialties.

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:dynamics-method}, a potential concern with cumulative windows is that attenuation may reflect compositional dilution rather than genuine behavioral change. We address this in two ways. First, the supplemental appendix decomposes each window’s links into persisting and newly formed ties. If attenuation were purely mechanical, new links in later windows would show concordance rates similar to those of persisting links. Instead, newly formed ties exhibit progressively lower organizational and demographic concordance across all three specialties. Second, the supplemental appendix presents period-specific (non-cumulative) estimates in which the model is estimated separately on referrals made in each individual post-move year. These estimates show a similar pattern of attenuation, providing further evidence that the decline reflects genuine behavioral change.


%%% MECHANISMS

\section{Peer Referrals and Practice Affiliation}
\label{sec:mechanisms}

Practice affiliation is the dominant predictor of referral formation across all three specialties, but this finding leaves open the question of how organizational ties translate into referral decisions. One possibility is that shared practice membership directly constrains referral choices through internal policies, shared electronic health records, or co-located offices. Another is that newly arrived PCPs learn about local specialists from their practice colleagues and adopt similar referral patterns. To shed light on this distinction, we construct a measure of peer referral behavior and examine whether it accounts for part of the practice affiliation effect.

For each mover, we measure the pre-arrival referral patterns of incumbent PCPs in the mover's new practice. Specifically, for each specialist in the mover's choice set, we count how many other PCPs in the same practice group referred to that specialist in the year before the mover arrived. This lagged measure captures the practice's established referral network at the time the mover enters, avoiding contamination from the mover's own referral decisions.

Table~\ref{tab:peer} presents the results across all three specialties using a conditional logit specification with PCP fixed effects. For each specialty, column (1) reproduces the baseline specification without peer referrals, column (2) adds the peer referral measure alongside practice affiliation, and column (3) drops practice affiliation entirely. The pattern is consistent across all three specialties. Adding peer referrals reduces the practice affiliation effect by roughly half in orthopedic surgery (from 6.6 to 3.2 percentage points) and cardiology (from 7.5 to 2.6 percentage points), and by nearly 80\% in dermatology (from 10.5 to 2.2 percentage points). The peer referral variable is positive and significant in all specifications, and its coefficient is largely unchanged when practice affiliation is dropped, suggesting that the two variables capture overlapping but not identical variation.

These results suggest that a substantial share of the practice affiliation effect operates through the referral behavior of incumbent colleagues. However, we note that peer referral patterns and organizational structure are not easily separable. If a practice has policies or norms that channel referrals to specific specialists, then all PCPs in the practice will exhibit similar referral patterns, and the peer referral variable will absorb what is fundamentally an organizational effect. The residual practice affiliation coefficient in column (2) captures the portion of organizational influence that operates beyond peer referral patterns, but the peer referral coefficient itself reflects a combination of information transmission and shared organizational channeling.


%%% WELFARE

\section{Quality Reallocation}
\label{sec:welfare}

Our results indicate that practice affiliation and geographic proximity are the dominant drivers of referral formation, raising a natural question for policy. Do these convenience-driven referral patterns come at a cost to patient outcomes? If PCPs are choosing specialists based on organizational ties rather than clinical quality, the welfare implications could be substantial, along the lines of the allocative inefficiency documented in \cite{chandra2020qje} for hospital-level treatment decisions. To gauge the magnitude of any such quality loss, we turn to a counterfactual exercise

Orthopedic surgery referrals culminate in discrete surgical episodes (major joint replacements) for which well-defined outcome metrics exist.\footnote{We construct a surgeon-level composite quality measure combining mortality, readmissions, and complications, drawing on quality measures developed under the CMS Comprehensive Care for Joint Replacement Model and the National Quality Forum. Some complications are identified only in the inpatient stay or only within 30 days, but no outcome measure extends beyond 90 days of discharge.} Cardiology and dermatology referrals, by contrast, typically lead to office-based consultations for evaluation rather than discrete procedures, making quality measurement substantially more difficult. We therefore restrict the quality analysis to orthopedic surgery, not because quality data are unavailable for the other specialties per se, but because the clinical setting permits a more credible and interpretable quality measure.

For each referral determinant, we set its estimated coefficient to zero, recompute referral probabilities using the two-stage predicted index, and calculate the change in expected adverse patient outcomes per 1,000 referrals within each HRR. Positive values indicate fewer expected adverse outcomes when the channel is removed, and negative values indicate worsening quality. This exercise parallels earlier work that attaches performance measures to observed physician networks \citep{barnett2012mc, landon_using_2013, zeltzer2020}.

The quality-reallocation effects (reported in full in the supplemental appendix) are generally modest. Practice affiliation and geographic distance produce the largest mean effects (0.27 and 0.29 fewer adverse outcomes per 1,000 referrals when each is zeroed out), while gender, race, age, and experience concordance produce mean effects near zero. Distance exhibits the widest cross-market dispersion (standard deviation of 4.65), with effects ranging from a worsening of roughly 4 adverse outcomes per 1,000 referrals at the 10th percentile to an improvement of more than 4 at the 90th percentile. Practice affiliation is more concentrated, with zero effect at both the 25th and 75th percentiles but a heavier right tail. These results suggest that the quality losses from homophily-driven referrals are small on average, though they may matter in specific local markets.


%%% DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This paper examines the determinants of initial referral networks between PCPs and specialists and how the determinants of referral networks evolve over time. We study three clinical areas with distinct institutional settings, namely orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and dermatology, using the 100\% Medicare FFS claims data from 2010 through 2018. Our identification strategy exploits PCP movers who relocate to a new market and must build their referral networks from scratch. We employ the quadruple-based estimator of \cite{jochmans2018}, which allows for both PCP and specialist fixed effects, together with a second-stage fixed-effect recovery procedure that enables marginal-effect calculations on the probability scale.

Across all three specialties, practice group affiliation is by far the largest contributor to initial link formation, with geographic proximity also emerging as a robust and persistent factor. Gender concordance is meaningful in orthopedic surgery but plays a smaller role in dermatology and is negligible for cardiology. Other measures of demographic concordance, including age, experience, and race, play at best a limited role. As PCPs spend more time in their new market, the effects of practice affiliation attenuate in all three specialties. Link decomposition and period-specific estimation confirm that this decline reflects genuine behavioral change rather than compositional dilution, consistent with organizational convenience playing a diminishing role as PCPs accumulate local information.

While we employ a mover-based identification strategy and rich econometric specification, allowing for both PCP and specialist fixed effects, our baseline results for practice affiliation and gender concordance in orthopedic surgery are broadly consistent with existing work \citep{zeltzer2020}, albeit with somewhat different magnitudes. By examining three specialties with distinct institutional settings and demographic compositions, we show that the dominance of organizational ties in referral formation is not specific to any single clinical area, though the magnitude varies across specialties.

We conclude with three policy observations. First, while increasing horizontal integration of physician practices can have large negative consequences with regard to competitiveness and physician pricing \citep{koch2021}, our results suggest that, insomuch as mergers alter physician referrals, the quality implications of such referral changes could be minor. 
Second, our results highlight the role of geographic proximity as a persistent factor in referral formation. Because referral choices are effectively constrained to a narrow geographic subset of specialists, demographic homophily has limited scope to affect patient outcomes. Whether the same would hold in settings where distance is less binding (e.g., telehealth or urban specialist density) is an open question for future work. Third, while organizational affiliation plays a dominant role in PCPs’ initial referral networks, these effects attenuate over time across all three specialties, suggesting that the initial convenience-driven network is not permanent and that PCPs do adjust their referral patterns as they gain local experience.
